What I Ask Myself

I keep a running collection of questions I ask myself. Not affirmations, not goals, not a journal prompt I found online once and never reused. Questions I come back to, on purpose, because the answer keeps changing and the changing is the point.

This post is the front door to that collection. It explains why I do this at all, how I actually run the practice, and what the little icons on every question mean.

Why ask yourself questions at all​

Most of my bad decisions came from never asking the question underneath the decision. I'd optimize how I was doing something for months before noticing I'd never asked whether it was worth doing. A good question is a flashlight: it doesn't answer anything for you, it just points at the part of the room you've been avoiding.

The practice is simple. I keep the questions grouped by what they're really about, I revisit a group when its season comes around, and I write the answer down so I can watch it move over the years. A question I answered one way at 25 I answer differently at 35, and the gap between those two answers tells me more than either answer alone.

How I run it​

A few rules I've settled on:

  • Group by what the question is really asking, not by where I found it. The grouping is part of the thinking.
  • Revisit on a cadence, not on a whim. Some questions are yearly. Some are for hard weeks only. The cadence is part of the question.
  • Write the answer down. An unrecorded answer can't be compared to next year's. The record is the whole point.
  • Keep the ones that sting. If a question is comfortable to answer, it's probably not doing any work.

The power of a question​

Not every question hits the same way. Some wake you up, some make you uncomfortable, some slowly reshape who you are. I mark each question with one or more power icons so you can see, at a glance, what kind of charge it carries. A calm everyday question gets no icon at all.

This is the legend. It's the same set of icons you'll see on every question across the series:

SparkA jolt that wakes you up
FireA burn that confronts you
ChiselCarves away what isn't you
AnvilReshapes who you become
·
CalmAn everyday question. No charge, still worth asking.

A question can carry more than one. "What is the purpose of your life?" both burns (Fire) and forges (Anvil): it's uncomfortable to sit with, and answering it honestly, year after year, changes who you are.

Alongside the power, each question card carries three quieter labels:

  • Priority (Core / High / Medium / Low). How foundational the question is.
  • Frequency (Yearly, Monthly, "In hard stretches"). When its season comes around.
  • Depth (Quick / Moderate / Deep). How much sitting-with it really demands.

Click any question to open the full card: why it matters, how often to ask it, when to ask it, and how often to write the answer down.
