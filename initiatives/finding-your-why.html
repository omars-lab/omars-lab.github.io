Starting With Why: Notes From Trying to Find My Own

September 27, 2017 · 4 min read
Omar Eid
Principal AI Architect & Entrepreneur

I read Simon Sinek's Start With Why at a point when I was trying to figure out my own. What follows isn't a summary of the book. It's the thinking it set off in me: the questions I kept circling, and the few ideas that reorganized how I look at companies, products, and myself. If the questions land, read the book.

Why before what​

The core inversion is simple and hard to live by: most people and companies know what they do and how they do it, but very few can say why. And the why is what actually moves people. A product is just proof of the why. People don't buy what you make so much as the reason you make it.

Once that clicks, you start reading companies differently. You can almost predict a company's future actions from its purpose, and you can ask a sharper diagnostic question about any of them: have its actions actually been in tune with its stated why, or has it drifted? I caught myself wanting to go down the list of the Fortune 500 and write the real why next to each one.

The pull of manipulation​

The other half of the book that stuck with me is the catalogue of manipulations, the levers companies pull when they don't have a why: promotions, fear, novelty, peer pressure. They work, but only for the transaction in front of you. They buy a sale, not loyalty.

It made me want to turn the lens on my own wanting. Why do I actually want a given thing? Because it's genuinely valuable to me, or to stop other people from judging me? How much of desire is just peer pressure wearing a nicer outfit? You can't build something honest until you can tell the difference in yourself.

What it means to be a "why" person​

Sinek splits the world into why types and how types, and the interesting claim is that they need each other: the why type supplies the cause and the direction, the how type builds the thing that makes it real. Neither is complete alone.

I spent a lot of these notes asking, bluntly, which one I am, and how you'd even become a why type if you weren't one. The honest answer I arrived at is that it starts with a discipline, not an epiphany: reminding yourself each morning why you got up and why you're doing the work, and documenting the moments you catch yourself thinking "wouldn't it be great if...", because those are the breadcrumbs that lead back to your purpose.

Purpose as the thing underneath the work​

The thread under all of it, for me, is that purpose isn't a marketing layer you add at the end. It's the thing the rest is built on. A leader's real job, the CEO's job, is to keep the why alive in what the company does, what it focuses on, and how it treats the world. When the why gets lost, the decline tends to follow.

The same is true one level down, for a person. I came out of this less interested in "what should I build" and more interested in "what do I actually believe, and what would I build because of it." That's a slower question. It's also the only one that seems to compound.

What I took from it​

Start With Why gave me a vocabulary for something I'd felt but couldn't name: that the most durable companies, and the most grounded people, run on a reason that comes before the product. Find the why. Be honest about manipulation, including your own. And treat purpose as infrastructure, not decoration.

Reflections on Start With Why by Simon Sinek.
