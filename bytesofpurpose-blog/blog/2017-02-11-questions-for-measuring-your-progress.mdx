Progress is easy to assume and hard to measure. It's worth stopping every so often to ask whether you're truly striving toward your potential, whether you can even tell if you're winning, and whether you have the will to keep going. These are the questions I sit with when I want an honest read on where I stand. Pick the ones that are uncomfortable to answer.

<!-- truncate -->

## Potential

<SectionBanner why="Potential is the gap between who you are and who you could be. These questions check whether you're actually moving across that gap or just standing at its edge." />

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Most people never approach the edge of what they're capable of. Asking whether you've even scratched the surface is a deliberately uncomfortable wake-up call."
  howOften="Quarterly, or whenever you suspect you're coasting"
  when="During a longer review of where your effort is actually going"
  record="Write an honest estimate of how much of your potential you're using">
  Have you even scratched the surface of your potential?
</Question>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Striving toward your potential is a direction, not a destination. Asking whether you're pursuing the person you could become keeps the effort aimed at growth rather than comfort."
  howOften="Quarterly, as a check on your trajectory"
  when="When reviewing whether your daily choices point toward who you want to be"
  record="Note where you're stretching and where you've settled">
  Are you striving to your potential? Are you pursuing becoming the person you can be?
</Question>

<Question

  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"

  when="When you feel stuck but haven't actually checked your position"

<Question
  power="fire"

  why="Progress is meaningless without a destination to measure it against. If you can't say where you're going, you can't tell whether you're getting closer."

## Winning and losing

<SectionBanner why="You can't tell whether you're winning if you've never defined the game or kept score. These questions force the scoreboard into view." />

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Wanting to win is not the same as winning, and not everyone actually wants it. This pairing checks both your position and your desire honestly."
  howOften="Monthly, taking the temperature of your effort and your want"
  when="When you're unsure whether you're committed or just going through motions"
  record="Write whether you're winning and whether you genuinely want to">
  Are you winning? Do you really want to win?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Without a scoreboard, you're guessing about your own progress. Keeping score is what makes winning and losing visible instead of a feeling."
  howOften="Monthly, checking whether you can even read your own results"
  when="When you can't say with confidence whether you're ahead or behind"
  record="Define what counts as a point and start tracking it">
  Are you keeping score? Can you tell if you are winning or losing?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="If losing costs you nothing, you won't fight to avoid it. Making losing painful is a way to raise your own stakes on purpose."
  howOften="Quarterly, when setting up the stakes for a goal"
  when="When you keep tolerating losses because they don't actually hurt"
  record="Write the consequence you'll attach to losing">
  How will you make losing painful?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="low"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Wins you never mark don't register as progress. Celebrating is how you make the scoreboard motivating instead of just informative."
  howOften="Monthly, and after any meaningful win"
  when="When you reach a milestone and are tempted to rush past it"
  record="Decide how you'll mark a win before you reach it">
  How do you celebrate?
</Question>

## Readiness and will

<SectionBanner why="Skill gets you to the starting line, but will is what carries you through the grind. These questions test whether you have the stomach for what progress actually demands." />

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Anything worth doing involves failing on the way there. Being ready to pursue failure, not just tolerate it, is what separates people who try from people who wish."
  howOften="Quarterly, and before starting anything where failure is likely"
  when="When you're about to commit to something hard enough to fail at"
  record="Write whether you're truly ready to fail in pursuit of this">
  Are you ready to pursue failure?
</Question>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'anvil']}
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="The romance of winning hides the grind that produces it: the emails, the rejection, the broken promises. Facing that burden honestly is the real readiness check."
  howOften="Quarterly, and before taking on a serious pursuit"
  when="When you're drawn to the prize but haven't reckoned with the cost"
  record="Write the unglamorous work the goal will actually require of you">
  Are you ready to take on the emotional burden of winning? Of sending the countless emails? Of dealing with rejection and broken promises?
</Question>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Will often beats raw skill over the long haul. Asking whether you're phenomenally willed reframes progress as a test of persistence, not just talent."
  howOften="Quarterly, when assessing what will carry you through"
  when="When skill alone clearly won't be enough to finish"
  record="Write an honest read on your will, separate from your talent">
  You may not be phenomenally skilled, but are you phenomenally willed?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Handing your state over to something you can't control is a quiet way to stall. Naming it is the first step to taking the wheel back."
  howOften="Monthly, scanning for where you've ceded control"
  when="When your progress feels hostage to something outside you"
  record="Name what's controlling you and what reclaiming control would look like">
  Are you letting something out of your control control you?
</Question>

## Goal achievement and tracking

<SectionBanner why="Progress you don't track is progress you can't trust. These questions build the measurement loop that turns effort into evidence." />

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Goals are only useful if you check whether you hit them. Asking what you've achieved closes the loop that goal-setting opened."
  howOften="Quarterly, reconciling goals against actual results"
  when="At the end of a quarter or goal period"
  record="Mark each goal as hit, missed, or in progress">
  Have you achieved any of your goals?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Task counts are a crude but real signal of throughput. Tracking them weekly keeps your sense of momentum tied to something countable."
  howOften="Weekly, as a simple throughput check"
  when="At the end of each week"
  record="Count completed tasks and watch the trend week over week">
  How many tasks did you complete this week?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The right report makes progress legible at a glance. Designing what to track is what turns scattered effort into a dashboard you can read."
  howOften="Monthly, or when your current view of progress feels murky"
  when="When you can't quickly see whether you're moving"
  record="Define the few reports or metrics that would show your progress clearly">
  What reports will help you track your progress?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Failing your way to success reframes setbacks as data points on the path, not detours off it. Asking whether you've begun is asking whether you're in the game."
  howOften="Monthly, checking your relationship with failure"
  when="When fear of failing is keeping you from starting"
  record="Note the failures you've already turned into forward steps">
  Have you begun failing your way to success?
</Question>
