Most reflection focuses on what you're doing and why. This set is about the *how*:
the processes, techniques, and systems you operate by without ever naming them. The
questions below are meant to surface those invisible methods so you can evaluate
them, reuse what works, and retire what doesn't. Pick a section and sit with it.

<!-- truncate -->

## Core processes

<SectionBanner why="The methods you run on are usually invisible until you name them. Putting language to how you actually operate is the first step to improving any of it." />

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="If you can't describe your own reflection process, you're reflecting by accident. Naming it lets you make it repeatable and reliable."
  howOften="Quarterly, or whenever your current way of reflecting stops producing insight"
  when="During a planning session or a quiet review of how you actually think things through"
  record="Write the steps out as a checklist you can follow next time">
  What is your self-reflection process?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Most problem-solving feels like improvisation, but there's usually a pattern underneath. Surfacing it lets you apply it on purpose instead of hoping it shows up."
  howOften="Quarterly, and after solving something hard, while it's fresh"
  when="Right after you work through a tricky problem and can still see the moves you made"
  record="Note the specific moves you made; the repeatable pattern is the technique">
  What is your problem-solving technique?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The mechanics of how you actually get to your outcomes are easy to leave unexamined. Naming the method tells you whether you're being deliberate or just lucky."
  howOften="Monthly, when reviewing what moved and what stalled"
  when="When something went your way and you want to understand why it worked"
  record="Write down the actual sequence that produced the result">
  How do you get what you want?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Your life runs on systems whether you designed them or not. Asking how it works lets you see the machinery instead of just living inside it."
  howOften="Quarterly, as a step back from the day to day"
  when="During a season change or any time the routine feels off"
  record="Sketch the loop: what comes in, what you do with it, what comes out">
  How does your life work?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="How you interpret a question shapes the answer you'll reach. If your understanding is sloppy, every downstream conclusion inherits the flaw."
  howOften="When a question feels confusing or you keep talking past someone"
  when="Before answering anything that matters, especially when stakes are high"
  record="Restate the question in your own words before you try to answer it">
  How do you understand questions?
</Question>

## Reusing and learning from the past

<SectionBanner why="Solutions you already found are wasted if you can't find them again. This section is about building a memory you can actually draw on." />

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Reinventing a solution you already discovered is pure waste. The leverage is in reaching back for what worked instead of starting from scratch."
  howOften="Monthly, when a new problem rhymes with an old one"
  when="When you hit something that feels familiar but you can't quite place"
  record="Keep a short log of solved problems and the approach that cracked each one">
  How will you reuse your solutions from previous problems to solve new ones?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="An answer you can't retrieve is the same as an answer you never had. Indexing is what turns past effort into a usable library."
  howOften="Monthly, as you accumulate notes and solutions"
  when="When your notes pile up and you can no longer find what you need"
  record="Pick a tagging or filing scheme and apply it consistently">
  How will you index your responses so you can find them again?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Old solutions go stale as your situation changes. Scheduling a re-look keeps you from running on advice that no longer fits."
  howOften="Quarterly is a reasonable default; tune it to how fast your context shifts"
  when="When setting your review cadence for the quarter"
  record="Set a recurring reminder and note what you actually changed each time">
  How often will you re-evaluate your previous solutions?
</Question>

## Process evaluation

<SectionBanner why="A process is only worth keeping if it earns its place. These questions audit what you run on so you can add what's missing and drop what's dead weight." />

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Gaps in how you operate often hide in plain sight. Asking what you're missing invites the upgrade you wouldn't otherwise notice."
  howOften="Quarterly, when reviewing how things are running"
  when="When something keeps slipping and no current process catches it"
  record="List one or two processes worth trying and what problem each would solve">
  Are there any new processes you would benefit from?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="A process you skip is a process you don't have. This is an honest check on whether you're actually doing the things you decided matter."
  howOften="Weekly, as part of a regular review"
  when="At the end of a week, looking back at what you intended versus what you ran"
  record="Mark which processes you ran and which you quietly dropped">
  Are you completing all of your processes, or failing to do any of them?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Reflecting on your processes is the meta-layer that keeps the rest honest. Without it, broken methods just keep running."
  howOften="Monthly, set aside for the question itself"
  when="During a monthly review or after a process clearly let you down"
  record="Write what's working, what isn't, and one change to try">
  How will you reflect on your processes?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Decisions about your processes tend to fade unless you record them. Tracking them keeps you from relitigating the same call over and over."
  howOften="Quarterly, when revisiting how you operate"
  when="After you commit to changing or keeping a way of working"
  record="Keep a running list of process decisions and the reason behind each">
  What decisions have you made regarding your processes?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Knowing what to do is different from having a way to start doing it. A process for coming into action is what closes the gap between intent and motion."
  howOften="When you have a plan but keep failing to start on it"
  when="When you're stalled at the threshold of a project you've already decided to do"
  record="Write the first concrete step and the trigger that kicks it off">
  What is your plan of action, your process for coming into action?
</Question>

## Artifacts and constraints

<SectionBanner why="A good process leaves something behind and runs inside limits. Naming the outputs and the boundaries keeps your methods grounded in something real." />

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="quick"
  why="A process that produces nothing tangible is hard to trust or improve. Naming the artifact gives you something concrete to point at and refine."
  howOften="Quarterly, or when designing a new process"
  when="When defining or revising how a piece of your work runs"
  record="Name the artifact each process should produce">
  What artifacts will your process produce?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="quick"
  why="Constraints aren't restrictions so much as guardrails that keep a process from sprawling. Choosing them on purpose makes the method sharper."
  howOften="When designing or tightening a process"
  when="When a process keeps expanding past where it's useful"
  record="Write the limits you'll hold the process to">
  What constraints will you impose on your process?
</Question>

## Automation

<SectionBanner why="Anything you do over and over by hand is a candidate for handing off to a system. This is where you reclaim time you didn't know you were spending." />

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Repetitive manual work is a slow leak of time and attention. Spotting what repeats is the first step to letting a system carry it instead."
  howOften="Monthly, scanning for the same task showing up again"
  when="When you catch yourself doing the same fiddly thing for the third time"
  record="List the repeating actions and which one is worth automating first">
  What actions repeat in your life that you can automate?
</Question>
