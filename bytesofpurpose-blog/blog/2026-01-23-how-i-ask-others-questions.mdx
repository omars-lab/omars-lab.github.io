I write a lot about the questions I ask myself. This is about the other half of the
practice: how I ask questions of other people. The two are related, but the skills
are different. A question I ask myself only has to be honest. A question I ask you
has to be honest *and* safe enough that you'll actually answer it.

<!-- truncate -->

## A question is an invitation, not a test

The fastest way to shut someone down is to ask a question they hear as a trap. "Why
did you do it that way?" is a real question, but most people hear an accusation. The
same curiosity, asked as "what were you optimizing for there?", opens the door instead
of guarding it.

So the first thing I watch is what the question *feels like* to receive. Before I ask,
I check: does this sound like I'm genuinely curious, or like I already have a verdict
and I'm collecting evidence? People can tell the difference instantly, and they answer
the version they think they heard.

## The questions that open people up

A few patterns I reach for:

- **Ask about the reasoning, not the result.** "What made that the right call at the
  time?" gets you the actual thinking. "Was that a good idea?" gets you a defense.
- **Ask for the story, not the summary.** "How did that start?" gives people room to
  walk you through it. They'll tell you things they'd never list if you asked directly.
- **Ask what they'd change.** "If you ran it again, what would you do differently?"
  invites reflection without forcing a confession. It assumes growth, not failure.
- **Leave silence after the question.** The most useful thing people say is usually the
  second thing, after the pause. If I fill the silence, I never hear it.

## The questions that close people down

The mirror image, the ones I try to catch myself before asking:

- **Leading questions** that are really statements wearing a question mark. "Don't you
  think we should just start over?" isn't a question. It's a position I'm asking you to
  ratify.
- **Stacked questions** where I ask three things at once and the person only answers the
  easiest one. One question at a time gets a real answer to each.
- **"Why" aimed at a person** instead of a decision. "Why are you like that?" is an
  attack. "Why does that approach work better?" is curiosity. Same word, opposite effect.

## Listening is part of the question

A question isn't finished when I stop talking. The other half is what I do with the
answer. If I ask something and then argue with the response, I've taught the person
that my questions aren't safe, and the next answer will be smaller and more careful.

So I try to earn the next honest answer with how I receive this one: take it seriously,
follow it where it goes, and let the person see that telling me the real thing didn't
cost them anything. That's what makes someone willing to be asked again.

## Where this connects

The inward practice and the outward one feed each other. The questions I've learned to
sit with myself are usually the ones I can ask other people without flinching, and the
answers other people trust me with often become questions I take back to
[ask myself](/thoughts/what-i-ask-myself). Curiosity, pointed in either direction, is
the same muscle.
