Knowing what I want sounds simple until I actually try to answer it. I chase plenty
of things I never deliberately chose. These are the questions I return to when I want
to cut through the noise and name what I'm really after, grouped by what they're
getting at. I sit with the ones that make me uncomfortable.

<!-- truncate -->

## Core desires

<SectionBanner why="Before I can chase anything well, I have to know what I actually want, and just as importantly what I don't. Naming both ends keeps me from spending a life pursuing things I never chose." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'chisel']}
  priority="core"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Most of what I want, I absorbed without choosing. Pairing 'what do you want' with 'what do you not want' forces the deliberate choice, because knowing what to refuse is half of knowing what to pursue."
  howOften="Once a year, and any time I feel like I'm chasing something on autopilot"
  when="During annual reflection, or when a goal I've been chasing suddenly feels hollow"
  record="Two lists, want and don't-want, written plainly; revisit and prune yearly">
  What do you want? What do you not want?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="core"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="This is the big, intimidating version of the question, and avoiding it is how decades slip by. Even a rough answer gives every smaller choice something to point at."
  howOften="Once a year, at minimum"
  when="At major crossroads: a new chapter, a hard decision, a season ending"
  record="Write a paragraph; it's allowed to change, but write something rather than nothing">
  What do you want to do with your life?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="'Rather have' is a trade-off question, and trade-offs are where real preferences show up. It cuts through wishful thinking by forcing me to give something up to get something else."
  howOften="Quarterly, when I'm weighing competing options"
  when="When I want two things and can't have both"
  record="Write the trade pairs: I'd rather have X than Y. The pattern reveals my real priorities">
  What would you rather have?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## What you're chasing

<SectionBanner why="There's a difference between what I claim to want and what I'm actually running toward all day. These questions catch what's really pulling me, including the infatuations and turn-offs I don't usually admit." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="What I'm chasing and what I'm working toward aren't always the same thing, and the gap between them is worth noticing. Naming the chase honestly shows whether my daily effort is aimed at what I actually want."
  howOften="Monthly, as a check on where my energy is going"
  when="When I feel busy but unsure whether the busyness is pointed anywhere"
  record="Write what I'm chasing this month and what I'm building toward; check whether they match">
  What are you chasing? What are you working towards?

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"

  depth="quick"
  why="Infatuation is a strong signal, even when it's a little embarrassing. What grabs me without my permission tells me something about my appetites that my considered goals won't admit."
  howOften="Whenever I notice I'm fixated on something"
  when="When I catch myself preoccupied with something I haven't consciously chosen"
  record="Just name it honestly; the point is awareness, not judgment">
  What are you infatuated with?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="What turns me on and off is raw motivational data. Tracking what energizes and what repels me, without dressing it up, shows me where to point my life and what to steer away from."
  howOften="Monthly, as a quick energy audit"
  when="When I want a gut-level read on what's actually pulling and pushing me"
  record="Two short columns, turned-on and turned-off, noted as I feel them">

</QuestionSection>

## Regrets and wishes

<SectionBanner why="Future regret is information about what actually matters to me, arriving early enough to do something about it. These questions borrow my deathbed perspective and bring it back to today." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'anvil']}
  priority="core"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Regret about what I didn't do is consistently heavier than regret about what I did. Asking what I'd be sorry I skipped pulls the most important things to the front before it's too late to act on them."
  howOften="Once a year, and any time I'm hesitating on something that matters"
  when="When I've been putting off something significant for months"
  record="Write the honest answer; if I'm not ready to act, write why, and whether that reason will hold in ten years">
  What would you be sorry you didn't do?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Looking back from the end and asking what I'd wish I had done is the same clarifier from a different angle. It bypasses my current excuses by speaking from the version of me who has run out of time to make them."
  howOften="Annually, as a deliberate thought experiment"
  when="During year-end reflection, or after losing someone and feeling the shortness of time"
  record="Write the wishes as a list, then circle the one I could start this month">
  What would you wish you did?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="'Regret not pursuing' is sharper than 'regret not doing,' because pursuit is about commitment and direction, not just a single action. It names the paths I'm quietly letting close without deciding to."
  howOften="Annually"
  when="When I notice a direction I keep almost-choosing but never commit to"
  record="Name the pursuits I'd regret abandoning; pick one to recommit to or consciously release">
  What would you regret not pursuing?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Dreams and aspirations

<SectionBanner why="Dreams point further than goals, and they carry a feeling I want others to share. These questions ask not just where I want to end up but whether I'm actually living toward it or just longing for it." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="A dream names the destination that goals only approximate. Pairing 'what is your dream' with 'where do you want to end up' keeps the dream from staying a vague feeling and makes it a place I can actually aim at."
  howOften="Once a year, written out fully"
  when="During annual reflection, or when I feel like I'm drifting without a horizon"
  record="Write the dream as a vivid picture, not a bullet point; the detail is what makes it pull">
  What is your dream? Where do you want to end up?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A vision that only matters to me is small. Asking what I want others to feel when they see it connects my dream to other people, which is usually where it gets its real weight and staying power."
  howOften="Annually, when refining the vision"
  when="When preparing to share the dream with others, or wondering whether it's bigger than me"
  record="Write the specific feeling I want to create in others, then check whether my dream actually delivers it">
  What do you want others to feel upon realizing your vision?
</Question>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'anvil']}
  priority="core"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="There's a difference between longing for a dream and living it, and this question is the gut-punch that checks which I'm doing. 'Crying for a dream' I'm not living is a sign to change something, not just to keep wishing."
  howOften="Once a year, and any time the gap between my life and my dream aches"
  when="When I feel the distance between the life I have and the life I want"
  record="Answer honestly whether I'm living the ideal or just mourning it; name one move to close the gap">
  Are you crying for a dream? Are you living your ideal life?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Emotions and triggers

<SectionBanner why="My strongest emotional reactions are arrows pointing at what I care about. What moves me to tears or sends a chill through me reveals desires my rational answers tend to hide." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="What makes me cry is rarely random; it touches something I deeply value. Tracking my emotional triggers is a back door into desires I can't always reason my way to."
  howOften="Whenever something moves me unexpectedly"
  when="After a moment that hit me harder than I expected"
  record="Note what triggered it and what value or longing sits underneath the reaction">
  What triggers your emotions? What has brought you to tears?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="low"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="quick"
  why="A chill or a shiver is the body answering before the mind can edit it. Those involuntary reactions mark the things that genuinely resonate, which makes them an honest signal of what I want."
  howOften="Whenever I feel that physical jolt of resonance"
  when="When a piece of music, an idea, or a moment gives me literal chills"
  record="Just note what caused it; the body is pointing at something worth following">
  What gives you the chills? What sends shivers down your spine?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Mastery and likes

<SectionBanner why="Wanting to be good at something and being willing to pay for it are two different things. These questions separate what I'd like to master from what I'm actually ready to sacrifice for, and remind me to notice the small likes too." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="What I want to master is a long-horizon commitment, and naming it keeps me from scattering my effort across things I only half-want. Mastery only happens where I'm willing to go deep, so the choice matters."
  howOften="Once a year; this should refine slowly rather than swing around"
  when="When deciding where to invest serious, sustained time"
  record="Name the craft I want to master; if I can't commit to one, that's useful information too">
  What do you want to master?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Mastery has a price, and wanting it isn't the same as being willing to pay. This question tests whether my desire to master something survives contact with what it actually costs in time, repetition, and discomfort."
  howOften="Annually, and before committing to a long climb"
  when="When I'm romanticizing being a master without facing the grind of becoming one"
  record="Write what mastery would actually require, then a yes or no on whether I'll pay it">
  Are you ready to do what it takes to become a master? What does it mean to be a master?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="The small things I like are easy to overlook, but they add up to a life. Keeping a running list of what I genuinely enjoy makes sure I build more of them in rather than only chasing the big ambitions."
  howOften="Monthly, as a light inventory"
  when="When I want to remember the simple things that make a day good"
  record="A running list of things I like; revisit it to make sure my life includes them">
  What are the things you like?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Infatuation and bliss

<SectionBanner why="The clearest sign I'm pointed at the right thing is the feeling of being lost in it. These questions check whether what I do actually absorbs me, or whether I just think it should." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="If I'm not infatuated with what I do, no amount of discipline will fully carry me through it. This question checks whether my work draws me in on its own, or whether I'm forcing an interest that isn't really there."
  howOften="Quarterly, as a check on my main work"
  when="When my work feels like a grind with no pull, or I'm questioning whether I'm in the right thing"
  record="Answer honestly; if the infatuation is gone, ask whether it ever existed or has just faded">
  Are you infatuated with what you do?
</Question>

<Question
  power={['spark', 'fire']}
  priority="core"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Following my bliss and noticing what I get lost in are the same signal: where my attention goes without being dragged. That self-sustaining absorption is the most honest evidence of what I actually want."
  howOften="Quarterly, and whenever I notice time disappearing on something"
  when="When I want to find the activities that absorb me completely"
  record="Note what I lose track of time doing; the list points straight at my bliss">
  Are you following your bliss? What are you lost in?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="It sounds basic, but I rarely stop to ask what actually makes me happy versus what I assume should. Checking it directly keeps my pursuit of big goals honest about whether they're delivering any joy along the way."
  howOften="Monthly, as a simple wellbeing check"
  when="When I'm chasing hard but can't remember the last time I felt genuinely happy"
  record="List what actually made me happy recently, not what I think should have">
  What makes you happy?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
