Most of the time we use questions to learn about the world. This set turns the lens
around and points it at your own reasoning. The goal isn't to win an argument with
yourself, it's to notice where you've stopped looking. Use these when you feel
unusually certain, when you're defending a position hard, or when you suspect you're
only collecting evidence that flatters what you already believe. Pick the ones that
make you a little uncomfortable.

## Identifying what to question

<SectionBanner why="Before you can challenge a belief you have to catch it in the act. These questions help me notice where my certainty is sitting so I know where to aim." />

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="You can't examine a belief you haven't named. This question makes me slow down and say out loud what I'm actually assuming, which is half the work."
  howOften="Weekly, or any time I notice I feel certain"
  when="When I'm about to make a decision based on something I've never questioned"
  record="Jot the assumption in a sentence so it stops being invisible">
  How do you question yourself? In what context are you questioning your life, your growth?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Naming the position I'm holding right now turns a vague feeling of certainty into something I can actually inspect. Most bad reasoning hides in assumptions I never bothered to state."
  howOften="Whenever I catch myself defending something hard"
  when="In the middle of a disagreement, or before committing to a plan"
  record="Write the position as a clear claim, then list what it quietly assumes">
  What are you assuming right now? What arguments or positions are you currently holding?
</Question>

## Challenging your thinking

<SectionBanner why="It is easy to collect evidence that flatters what I already believe. This cluster forces me to go looking for the parts that hurt." />

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="If I'm only finding evidence that agrees with me, I'm not thinking, I'm decorating. Actively hunting for discomforting evidence is the cheapest way to catch a blind spot before it costs me."
  howOften="Any time I feel a strong conviction forming"
  when="When the evidence has been suspiciously one-sided"
  record="List the strongest piece of evidence against your own view">
  Are you looking for discomforting evidence?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="core"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="Assuming I might be wrong is the single most useful habit I have. It keeps me curious instead of defensive, and it is the difference between learning and just being loud."
  howOften="On any decision that matters"
  when="Before I commit, especially when I'm most sure"
  record="Write the specific way you could be wrong, not a vague disclaimer">
  Are you considering where you are wrong? How might you be wrong?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="My conclusions are only as good as my inputs. Interrogating the information itself (its source, its freshness, its bias) catches errors upstream of my reasoning."
  howOften="When a conclusion rests on a single source or stat"
  when="Before citing or acting on information I haven't vetted"
  record="Note where the information came from and what it might be missing">
  What problems do you have with the information you're relying on?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Attachment to an argument is usually about ego, not truth. Asking why I'm so adhered to it separates 'this is correct' from 'I don't want to lose.'"
  howOften="When I notice I'm getting heated"
  when="When defending a position feels personal"
  record="Write the honest reason you're holding on, then ask if it's a reason or an excuse">
  Why are you so adhered to your argument?
</Question>

## Considering opposing views

<SectionBanner why="The fastest way to test a belief is to genuinely understand its opposite. These questions make me argue the other side as if I meant it." />

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="If I can't state the opposite side fairly, I don't actually understand my own position, I just inherited it. Steelmanning the other view is the real test of whether I've thought it through."
  howOften="On any contested belief"
  when="Before I dismiss a view I disagree with"
  record="Write the best version of the opposing argument, not the weakest">
  Have you heard the opposite side of the argument?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A position can feel right and still be built on a logical gap. Hunting for flaws in my own structure is more reliable than waiting for someone else to find them for me."
  howOften="When the stakes of being wrong are high"
  when="Before presenting or acting on a conclusion"
  record="Name the weakest link in your own reasoning chain">
  Does your position contain any logical flaws?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Looking directly at the evidence that contradicts me is uncomfortable, which is exactly why it works. The contradicting evidence is where I'm most likely to be wrong and least likely to look."
  howOften="On any strong conviction"
  when="When I've been avoiding a particular fact or counterexample"
  record="Write down the contradicting evidence plainly, without softening it">
  What evidence contradicts or challenges your position?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="If a single honest conversation couldn't move me at all, my belief isn't a conclusion, it's a wall. This question measures how open I actually am versus how open I claim to be."
  howOften="When I notice I'm unwilling to budge"
  when="In the middle of a disagreement that feels stuck"
  record="Estimate the odds honestly, then ask what evidence would actually change them">
  What are the odds of your mind changing through a simple conversation?
</Question>

## Examining your reasoning

<SectionBanner why="These overlap on purpose: assumptions, evidence, and error are the three places reasoning quietly fails. Returning to them is a deliberate audit of the foundation." />

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="Every conclusion sits on assumptions I usually never state. Dragging them into the open is the only way to check whether the foundation actually holds the weight I've put on it."
  howOften="On any belief that drives a real decision"
  when="When I'm reasoning from something that 'everyone knows'"
  record="List the assumptions explicitly, then mark which ones you've actually tested">
  What assumptions underlie your thinking?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Holding the supporting and the challenging evidence side by side keeps me honest. It stops me from counting the wins and ignoring the losses."
  howOften="Before reaching a firm conclusion"
  when="When I've only tallied the evidence on one side"
  record="Make two columns: what supports the view and what challenges it">
  What evidence supports your position, and what evidence challenges it?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="core"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="This is the question I most want to skip, which is why I keep it. Genuinely sitting with how I might be wrong is the practice that keeps everything else honest."
  howOften="On every belief that matters"
  when="When I feel most certain"
  record="Write the most plausible way you're wrong, and what you'd do if you were">
  How might you be wrong about this?
</Question>

## Categories worth questioning

<SectionBanner why="When I can't tell what I've taken for granted, I run my thinking through these lenses one by one. Each one surfaces assumptions I'd otherwise never notice." />

When you're not sure where to aim, run through these lenses and ask what you've taken
for granted in each.

- Setting: the story you're telling, the location, the interactions
- Subjects: people and relationships
- Role models and the abstractions you're reasoning from
- Mood, emotions, celebration, kindness
- Necessity and circumstances
- Action, habits, time, buying habits, change
- Improvement and growth
- Concerns and worries
- Usability, accessibility, affordability
- Experience and memories
- Assumptions, false assumptions, problems, issues, insecurities
- Motivation and inspiration
- Likes, desires, dislikes
