Most of us were taught *what* to learn, but rarely *how* we ourselves learn best. Getting deliberate about your own process changes everything: how you find a topic, how you absorb it, and how you know when you've actually got it. The questions below walk through learning style, process, seeking knowledge, expanding it, the methods worth studying, and how to keep the whole thing in motion.

<!-- truncate -->

## Your learning style

- How do I learn? What do I need to personally do to learn something?
- What resources do I use to learn?
- How many books do I need to read on a topic before I have learned it?
- Can I really learn on my own? What am I good at learning on my own?
- What are the common questions I ask when attempting to learn a new field?

## Your learning process

- What do I need to continually do to ensure that I am always in a state of learning?
- What does my personal learning process look like? A starting shape:
  - Capture a whole bunch of questions that intrigue me around a topic
  - Formulate a backlog to learn more about the topic
  - Come up with fun projects that may overlap with learning about one or more topics
  - As needed, come up with a plan for longer backlogs
  - Capture repetitive tasks

## Seeking knowledge effectively

A guiding principle: employ the scientific method as much as possible.

- Why do I hunger for a certain piece of knowledge?
  - Identify my intellectual gaps
  - Identify my spiritual gaps
- After figuring out a topic I wish to learn more about, study the big names in the field:
  - Find books that they wrote
  - Seek projects that were worked on in the same context
  - Read books, listen to lectures, watch videos
- While observing:
  - Take notes; document thoughts and ideas
  - Structure notes; make graphs
  - Learn the setup (you don't have to like the setup)

## Expanding your knowledge

1. Map what I know and what I don't
2. Identify all the areas of research that overlap with my interests
3. Classify all the books I run into
4. Figure out what I intend to read and for what purpose
5. Identify an area in which I wish to grow and identify the books that will best help me achieve that goal
6. Take notes while reading
7. Expand the concepts I am aware of
8. Track all the books I've read, and mark the pages that stand out

## Learning methods

- What are different ways in which I can learn?
- Topics worth learning more about:
  - Socratic Method of Learning (ask questions and find answers)
  - Instructional Design (crafting courseware to maximize how people learn)
  - Bloom's Taxonomy: kinesthetic (do), knowledge (know), affective (feel)
  - "Education is to understand complexity; training is to understand function"

## Maintaining a learning mindset

- How do I stay in a state of continuous learning?
- Am I always being a sponge? Am I making sure to never stop learning?
- When do I get bored, and when do I stop learning?

## Self-teaching vs needing a teacher

- What do I need a teacher to learn versus what can I learn on my own?
- Why do I need a teacher?

## Understanding knowledge

- Where do the different sciences intersect?
- What is the ultimate goal of each of the sciences?
- How can some sciences benefit from other sciences?
- What tools can be used across sciences?

## Capturing your learnings

- How do I capture my learnings, and where?
- How will I organize and keep track of what I want to learn?
- What have I already learned? What notes have I captured?
- What gotchas have I encountered?
- What diagrams will I create to capture my learnings?
- Where do I capture the things I learned about the different tools I have used?
- Where do I capture the things I learned about different software components?
