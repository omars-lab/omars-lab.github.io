Growth is easy to assume and hard to verify. It's possible to stay busy for years without getting meaningfully better at anything. These are the questions I sit with when I want to know whether I'm truly improving, where I'm investing my energy, and whether I'm living up to what I'm capable of. Pick the ones that make you a little uncomfortable.

<!-- truncate -->

## Growth mindset

<SectionBanner why="Growth starts as a posture before it shows up as progress. These questions check whether I actually want to get better, or just want to feel like I am." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="core"
  cron="daily"
  depth="quick"
  why="The daily intention to improve compounds in a way that occasional bursts never do. Wanting to get a little better every single day is the simplest engine of real growth."
  howOften="Daily, as a standing commitment"
  when="At the start of each day"
  record="Note one thing you got better at today, however small">
  Do you want to get better, every single day?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Busy is not the same as growing. Distinguishing real change from mere activity catches the years that feel productive but leave me exactly where I started."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When I feel busy but can't point to what's actually changed"
  record="Write concrete evidence that you're different than last month">
  Are you changing? Are you growing? Are you improving?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Growth has a price, and naming the hard work I'm willing to do separates ambition from wishful thinking. The work I'll actually commit to is the real measure of how much I want it."
  howOften="Weekly, when planning effort"
  when="When setting goals that sound nice but require real labor"
  record="Name the specific hard work, not the comfortable version">
  What hard work will you do?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="At the end of any chapter, the question is whether I gained more than I started with. Asking it in advance keeps me from coasting through a season and arriving empty-handed."
  howOften="Yearly, or at the end of a chapter"
  when="When wrapping up a long effort or period"
  record="Project what you'll walk away with, then check it against reality later">
  When it's all over, will you walk away with more than what you had?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Investing in yourself

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"

  record="Name a specific investment in yourself for the coming month">

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"

  How much knowledge are you inputting into yourself?

<Question

  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When my methods feel limited or stale"
  record="List techniques you know versus the few you actually depend on">
  What techniques are you aware of? What techniques do you depend on?

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"

  when="When setting the direction for a stretch of effort"
  record="Define the concrete result you want from your investment">
  What results are you looking for?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Self-knowledge for growth

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="high"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"

  when="When choosing how to learn or where to specialize"
  record="Name the specific ways you're smart, not the generic ones">

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"

  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When deciding what's worth growing toward"
  record="List what you actually care about, then check your effort goes there">
  What do you care about?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="My values quietly govern every choice, including how I grow. Making them explicit lets me grow on purpose instead of being steered by values I never examined."
  howOften="Yearly"
  when="During deep reflection on my direction"
  record="Write the values that actually drive your choices, not the aspirational ones">
  What are the values that motivate your choices?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"

  record="Estimate your rate of improvement, then check if it's rising or flat">

## Learning from masters

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"

  howOften="Quarterly"

  record="List the techniques the masters used and which you could adopt">

<Question

  howOften="Quarterly"

  record="Name the masters in your target area and what makes them masters">
  Who has mastered what you're trying to learn?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Measuring growth

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="chisel"

## Acknowledging limits

<SectionBanner why="Honest growth starts with an honest map of what I don't know. These questions keep me humble about the edges of my knowledge so I don't build on ground that isn't there." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="The limits of my foreknowledge are where overconfidence lives. Naming what I can't actually predict keeps me from betting too much on a future I can't see clearly."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When making a confident prediction about the future"
  record="Write what you genuinely can't foresee about this situation">
  What are the limits of your foreknowledge?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Acknowledging the limits of my knowledge is the prerequisite for filling them. Pretending to know more than I do quietly blocks the very learning that would close the gap."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When I'm tempted to act like I know more than I do"
  record="Name the edges of your knowledge in this area, plainly">
  Are you acknowledging the limits of your knowledge?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="My actions have a reach, and knowing where it ends keeps my effort focused on what I can actually affect. Pushing against immovable limits just drains the energy I need for the movable ones."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When I'm spending effort on things outside my control"
  record="Mark what's within your power to change and what isn't">
  Are you acknowledging the limits of your actions?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
