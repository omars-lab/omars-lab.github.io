Priorities only become real when they meet a calendar. Planning is how I translate "what matters" into time-bound action across horizons: today, this week, this quarter, this year, and the whole arc of a life. Use the questions below as prompts. Pick the horizon you're working in, and answer honestly rather than aspirationally.

<!-- truncate -->

## Understanding time and planning

<SectionBanner why="Before I plan a single day, I need to understand what planning even is and how I actually spend my time, not how I imagine I do." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="There's a sharp difference between what I think I should spend time on and what I actually spend it on. Facing the real answer is where honest planning starts."
  howOften="Weekly, as an honest audit of where time went"
  when="During a weekly review of the past week"
  record="Write where my time actually went, not where I meant it to go">
  This is NOT *what should I spend my time on*, but rather *what do I actually spend my time on*. How am I spending my time?
</Question>

<Question
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="If I don't know what makes a good plan or how often to make one, every plan I write is a guess about its own quality."
  howOften="When establishing or revisiting my planning cadence"
  when="When deciding what and how often to plan"
  record="Note what a good plan must address and how often I replan">
  What should be planned? How are we planning for the future? What makes a good plan? What questions should a plan address? How often should I plan?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="Untangling whether prioritizing is part of planning, or a step before it, clarifies the whole pipeline from wants to scheduled action."
  howOften="When my planning and prioritizing feel tangled together"
  when="During reflection on how my process fits together"
  record="Write my own definition of planning and where prioritizing sits in it">
  Is prioritizing a part of planning? Or do I figure out my priorities first, and then figure out how to collectively realize them? As a do-er, I grow a list of things I want to do; as a planner, I figure out what I should do first. What is planning? What does it mean to plan?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

A few framing ideas worth holding onto:

- Planning is part of time management; it is preparing for a future.
- A plan is a sequential set of steps to help everyone reach a common goal.
- If we don't all adhere to the same plan, we may step on each other's toes.
- A plan considers priorities and chains them together.

*Further reading:* [Time management vs. task management](https://toggl.com/blog/time-management-vs-task-management) and [Planning in time management](https://www.managementstudyguide.com/planning-in-time-management.htm).

## Knowing why you're doing what you're doing

<SectionBanner why="A plan without a reason behind each item is just a schedule. I want to always be able to justify what I'm doing and what I'm choosing not to do." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="daily"
  depth="moderate"
  why="If I can't justify why I'm doing this and not something else, I'm probably running on autopilot rather than intention."
  howOften="Daily, and any time someone could reasonably ask for a status"
  when="At any point during the day when I question my focus"
  record="Be able to state, in a sentence, what I'm working on and why">
  Why am I doing what I am doing, and can I justify why I am not doing something else? At a given point in time, what am I working on? As a programmer, what features am I working on? As a learner, what am I learning? As a doer, what projects am I working on?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

I don't know when I'll be asked for a status update, but I need to be confident and clear, because I should always know what I am doing and why I am doing it.

## Recurring activities

<SectionBanner why="Across all my horizons, the same handful of activities keep showing up. Naming them tells me what my time really goes toward, week after week." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="The things I constantly find myself doing are the true shape of my life. Sorting them by type shows whether I'm growing, fixing, or just spinning."
  howOften="Weekly, when reviewing how I spent my effort"
  when="During a weekly reflection"
  record="Tally my recurring activities by category (learning, doing, organizing, fixing, marketing)">
  What are the things I constantly find myself doing? Which of these am I doing: learning something new, doing something interesting, organizing myself, fixing a gap or weakness, or marketing myself?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Daily planning

<SectionBanner why="The shortest horizon. A day is where intention either turns into action or quietly evaporates, so I name what today is actually for." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="daily"
  depth="quick"
  why="A day without a named plan defaults to whatever's loudest. Stating today's intent keeps me steering instead of reacting."
  howOften="Daily, first thing"
  when="At the start of each day"
  record="A short, concrete plan for the day">
  What should I accomplish today? What is my plan for today?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Weekly planning

<SectionBanner why="A week is the unit where I can both refine the last stretch and set up the next. It's the rhythm where most of my real adjustments happen." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Planning a week as a connected arc (refine, introduce, align toward next week) is what keeps weeks from being seven disconnected days."
  howOften="Weekly, on the weekend"
  when="When kicking off the upcoming week"
  record="Note what to refine, what to introduce, and how this week sets up the next">
  What should we refine from last week? What should we introduce this week? What are we planning on doing next week? How should we align what we do this week to make next week easier to work with?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Quarterly planning

<SectionBanner why="A quarter is long enough to make real progress and short enough to course-correct. It's where weekly motion connects to yearly ambition." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Quarterly objectives are the bridge between this week and this year. If a quarter doesn't ladder up to the year, the year won't happen by accident."
  howOften="Quarterly, at the start of each quarter"
  when="When setting objectives for the new quarter"
  record="Name the quarter's objectives and how they feed the yearly goals; snapshot my learning plans">
  What should I accomplish this quarter? What are my quarterly objectives? How does this quarter contribute to my yearly goals? Have I snapshotted my learning plans so I know what I've committed myself to learn over time?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Yearly planning

<SectionBanner why="A year is long enough to change who I am, not just what I finish. This horizon is where I name what I want to be different by next year." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="What I commit to this year, and what I deliberately do differently, shapes the person I become. A vague year produces a vague version of me."
  howOften="Once a year, during annual planning"
  when="At the start of a new year"

  What must I accomplish this year? What will I do differently this year? What books will I read, scripts will I write, and topics will I learn this year? This year, it will be valuable if I…
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Lifetime timeline

<SectionBanner why="The widest horizon. Zooming out to decades reveals the trajectory my daily choices are quietly drawing, and whether it points where I want to go." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'anvil']}
  priority="core"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Most people never look this far out, so their life's arc is set by default. Naming the trajectory and its milestones turns drift into direction."
  howOften="Once a year, as a dedicated long-view exercise"
  when="During deep annual reflection"
  record="Sketch where I see myself across 30, 20, 10, 5, and 1 year, and the milestones in between">
  Where do I see myself in 30 years, 20 years, 10 years, 5 years, 1 year? What is the trajectory of my life? What major milestones do I want to hit?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Planning techniques

<SectionBanner why="Knowing the horizons isn't enough; I need techniques for replanning and for sizing a learning plan. These are the mechanics that make the horizons usable." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A replanning rhythm (weekly kickoffs, a carry-over versus new-work view, quarterly work-and-learning plans) keeps plans alive instead of stale on day three."
  howOften="Weekly for replanning, quarterly for the deeper plan"
  when="On the weekend for the week, and at quarter start for the bigger plan"
  record="Keep a weekly Venn of new versus carried-over work, and publish what I do each week">
  How often do I "replan"? (Weekly: put together weekly plans and kick them off on the weekend. Keep a Venn diagram each week of how much new stuff versus how much carries over. Publish the things I do weekly. Quarterly: plan for work and learnings.)
</Question>

<Question
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Sizing a learning plan into fixed chunks (10-week blocks, with a daily time budget) makes 'I want to learn X' into something a calendar can actually hold."
  howOften="Quarterly, when building a learning plan"
  when="When committing to learn something over a real stretch of time"
  record="Define the chunk size, what I can learn in 10 weeks, and the daily time I'll spend">
  How much time should be considered in each chunk of the plan? A learning plan should be in 10-week chunks: what can I learn in 10 weeks? How much time will I spend each day?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Reflecting on and assessing plans

<SectionBanner why="A plan I never check against reality is a wish. These questions close the loop: am I on track, and when do I adjust, re-prioritize, or change course entirely?" />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Without periodic honesty about whether I'm on track, I can stay busy on a path that no longer serves me. Reflection is the steering, not the trip."
  howOften="Monthly for progress, quarterly for trajectory"
  when="During monthly review and quarterly re-evaluation"
  record="Note whether I'm on track, what to adjust, and how often I'll re-check">
  Am I on track with my plan? What adjustments need to be made? How do I reflect on my plans? How often are my learning checkpoints, and how often do I re-evaluate my learnings, assess my trajectory, generate progress reports, and re-prioritize?
</Question>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="The hardest planning question is when to abandon a path for a better one. Asking it deliberately keeps sunk cost from steering my future."
  howOften="Quarterly, or whenever a path stops paying off"
  when="When deciding whether to continue or switch direction"
  record="Write the case for staying versus switching, judged against everything else I could do">
  When do I decide whether to continue down this path, or switch to something more beneficial for the time? Are the things I am pursuing the best things to learn right now, considering all the things I could be doing? What functionality is valuable, what is my function to society, and what am I passionate about producing versus consuming?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
