Before I can decide what to work on now, I have to see the whole field of what I *could* do. Prioritizing too early quietly throws away options I never even named. These questions are for the capture step: brainstorming and organizing my goals, commitments, challenges, and the things I want to build across every domain of my life, before any of it gets ranked.

<!-- truncate -->

## What do you want to do?

<SectionBanner why="The first job of capture is to empty my head onto the page. Anything that stays unnamed can't be chosen later, so this section casts the widest possible net before any sorting begins. I record goals in the context of my roles." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="The things I've idly thought about doing are the raw material for everything else. If I don't capture them when they surface, they evaporate."
  howOften="Weekly, or whenever an idea pops up"
  when="During a brainstorm or capture session"
  record="Keep a running list; nothing is too small to note">
  What have I thought about doing?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Naming my distinct streams of work shows me how my effort is actually divided. It's easy to think I have one focus when I really have five competing ones."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When my attention feels split across too many fronts"
  record="List each stream of work as its own line">
  What are my different streams of work?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The whole point of capture is to feed this question well. Once everything is on the table, asking what to do with my time has real options to choose from."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When planning where the week's hours go"
  record="Pick from your captured list, don't invent on the spot">
  What should I do with my time?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Most options are not equally valuable. Forcing the most-valuable-now question keeps capture from becoming an excuse to do everything at once."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When choosing the next thing to actually start"
  record="Name the single highest-value option and why">
  What's the most valuable thing I can do now?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Capturing what I could do is only half of it; the other half is doing it efficiently. This question pushes me to use the time I have, not just plan around it."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When reviewing how the week's time was spent"
  record="Note where time leaks and where it could compound">
  How can I make the most out of my time?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Initiatives are the bundles my scattered ideas group into. Naming them, and the reason each one matters, turns a pile of wants into a small set of things I can stand behind."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When organizing captured ideas into bigger themes"
  record="List each initiative, then write why it's important">
  What initiatives do I have? Why do I think each of these initiatives is important?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A goal without the tasks under it is just a slogan. Counting the tasks per objective shows me which goals are actually fleshed out and which are still hollow."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When breaking goals down into actionable work"
  record="For each objective, list its tasks and count them">
  What must be done in order to realize my goals? How many tasks do I have in each objective?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Goals by category

<SectionBanner why="Capturing goals one domain at a time keeps any area of life from being silently skipped. Walking the categories deliberately, from career to spiritual, makes the blind spots obvious." />

<QuestionSection>

### Career

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Career goals drift if I never name them. Writing them down, including the skills and the position I'm aiming for, gives my work somewhere to point."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="During a review of where my career is heading"
  record="Write the goal, the skills it needs, and the role you're aiming for">
  What are my career goals? What skills do I want to attain? What position do I want to reach?
</Question>

### Family

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Family goals rarely show up on a task list, so they get crowded out by work that does. Naming them protects the part of life that matters most but shouts least."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When checking that family isn't being squeezed out"
  record="Write what you want to accomplish for and with your family">
  What are my family goals? What do I want to accomplish for my family?
</Question>

### Financial

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Money goals are easy to leave vague, which makes them easy to ignore. Naming concrete milestones turns a fuzzy hope into something I can track."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When reviewing financial direction"
  record="Write the goal plus specific, dated milestones">
  What are my financial goals? What financial milestones do I want to reach?
</Question>

### Growth

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Personal growth is the category most likely to stay abstract. Asking which aspects to improve, whether daily work is needed, and what recurring action each demands turns a wish to be better into an actual practice."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When designing or reviewing habits"
  record="For each aspect, note whether it needs daily work and the recurring action it implies">
  How do I want to grow as a person? What aspects of myself do I want to improve? Do I need to improve these aspects of me on a daily basis? If so, what recurring action do I need to take for each aspect?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Even within growth, I can't work on everything. This question forces me to narrow to the few areas that truly deserve focus right now."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When too many growth fronts are competing"
  record="Name the small set you'll truly focus on this month">
  What sets of things should I truly currently be focusing on?
</Question>

### Health

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Health is the base everything else runs on, and the first thing sacrificed under pressure. Naming the goals keeps it from being an afterthought."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When reviewing physical well-being"
  record="Write the health goals and how you'll improve physical well-being">
  What are my health goals? How do I want to improve my physical well-being?
</Question>

### Learning

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="There's far more I could learn than I have time for. Capturing the wants, then choosing what to focus on, keeps learning intentional instead of random."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When deciding what to study next"
  record="List what you want to learn, then mark what you've chosen to focus on">
  What do I want to learn? What skills do I need to develop? Of the things I could possibly learn about, what have I chosen to focus on?
</Question>

### Spiritual

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Spiritual goals shape who I'm becoming at the deepest level, but they're the easiest to leave unspoken. Naming them gives that growth a direction."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="During reflection on spiritual direction"
  record="Write the spiritual goals and how you want to grow">
  What are my spiritual goals? How do I want to grow spiritually?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Commitments

<SectionBanner why="Commitments are the goals I've already bound my time to, which makes them the truest signal of my real priorities. This section audits whether what I've committed to still matches what I care about." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="deep"
  why="A commitment is a goal I've physically committed my time to, which is different from one I merely hold. Listing them and checking whether they reflect my priorities is where the honesty lives, because commitments don't lie about where my hours go."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When auditing where my time actually goes"
  record="List each commitment; mark whether it reflects a real priority">
  What are my commitments? What have I physically committed my time to doing? Do my commitments reflect my priorities? How does this differ from my goals?
</Question>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="A commitment that will stick with me until I die is a different weight than a passing one. Naming the lifelong ones clarifies what I've actually built my identity around."
  howOften="Yearly"
  when="During deep reflection on lasting commitments"
  record="Name the commitments meant to last a lifetime">
  What commitments have I made that will stick with me until I die?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Initiatives and commitments are easy to confuse, but they aren't the same: an initiative is something I want to do, a commitment is something I've bound myself to. Keeping the distinction clear keeps my capture honest."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When organizing the difference between wants and obligations"
  record="Note which captured items are initiatives versus commitments">
  Are initiatives the same as commitments?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A commitment has a shape: when it started, how long it lasts, and how much daily time it eats. Knowing those details tells me what I can realistically add or must drop."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When checking whether I'm honoring my commitments"
  record="For each commitment, note its start date, duration, daily cost, and whether you're keeping up">
  When did I make each of my commitments? Am I keeping up with each of my commitments? How long does each of my commitments last for? How much of my daily time does each commitment consume?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A commitment usually exists to advance some personal goal. Tracing each one back to the goal it partially realizes keeps me from honoring obligations that no longer serve anything I want."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When tying commitments back to underlying goals"
  record="For each commitment, note which personal goal it partially realizes">
  Which of my personal goals do I attempt to partially realize through my commitments?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Sorting commitments by who they're to, annual, family, self, spiritual, reveals whether any whole category is being neglected. The gaps are usually the relationships I claim matter most."
  howOften="Yearly"
  when="When checking that no category of commitment is empty"
  record="Group commitments by who they're to; look for empty categories">
  What are my annual commitments? What commitments have I made to my family? What commitments have I made to myself? What commitments have I made spiritually?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Challenges

<SectionBanner why="I need to consistently create new challenges to keep myself engaged. This section captures the hard things I'm choosing on purpose, because comfortable goals rarely grow me." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Challenge is what keeps work from going stale. Asking how I'll challenge myself, and how I already am, keeps me reaching just past my current edge instead of coasting."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When the work has started to feel too comfortable"
  record="Name the challenge you'll set and the one you're already in">
  How will I challenge myself? How am I challenging myself? What will I push myself to do?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Motivation and difficulty point at the same target from two sides. Capturing what I'm motivated to do alongside what feels hard reveals the difficult things worth attempting."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When deciding which hard things are worth the effort"
  record="List what motivates you and the hard goals you want to reach">
  What am I motivated to do? What difficult things do I want to accomplish?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Naming the challenges I'm facing and the milestones that feel out of reach makes them concrete. A vague sense of difficulty is paralyzing; a named obstacle can be planned around."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When something feels too hard to start"
  record="List current challenges and the milestones that seem hard to reach">
  What challenges am I facing? What milestones seem hard to reach?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Things to build (artifactual outcomes)

<SectionBanner why="Some goals produce a thing I can point at: an app, a site, a document. Capturing those artifacts separately keeps the tangible outputs from getting lost among abstract aims." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Building something produces a concrete artifact, which is satisfying and shareable in a way abstract goals aren't. Capturing what I want to make keeps the tangible outputs on my radar."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When brainstorming things to create"
  record="List the apps, sites, diagrams, or documents you want to produce">
  What do I want to build/create? What artifacts do I want to produce? What apps, sites, diagrams, or documents do I want to make?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="low"

  depth="deep"

## Why nots

<SectionBanner why="The fastest way to find missed options is to ask why I'm not already doing something. This section deliberately hunts for the things I've overlooked rather than the ones already on the list." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The 'why don't I' question is a deliberate provocation. It surfaces the things I've dismissed without examining, which is where overlooked opportunities tend to hide."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When the option list feels too safe or too short"
  record="List the things you're NOT doing that deserve a second look">
  Why don't I...? What am I NOT doing that I should consider?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Reflecting on your initiatives

<SectionBanner why="Once initiatives are captured, they deserve a second look: what goals hide inside them, why I want them, and how they're shifting over time. Capture without reflection just produces a longer list." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Initiatives often contain goals I never stated explicitly, and the reasons I want each thing change as I do. Digging into the hidden goals and tracking how my wants shift keeps the capture honest over time."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When reviewing how my interests are evolving"
  record="For each initiative, name the hidden goal, the reason, and how it's changed">
  What goals are hidden in my initiatives? Why do I want to do each of these things? As time has progressed, how are the things I want to work on changing?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Identifying projects

<SectionBanner why="A project is a bundle of work with a definite outcome, and it's the unit I actually execute on. This section turns loose ideas and learning objectives into projects worth committing to. Innovating, for me, is taking an idea and merging it with what I'm passionate about until it becomes a project bundle I can work on." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A project needs a defined end: an artifact, a change in state. Naming the potential projects and their outcomes is what separates real bundles of work from vague intentions."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When turning ideas into concrete projects"
  record="For each project, write the end result and the reason to do it">
  What projects can I potentially work on? What bundle of work can I do? What is the end result / artifact / change in state of each project? Why should I work on each project?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="There's a real method to going from a raw idea to a workable project. Naming how I refine ideas and learning objectives into project bundles makes that process repeatable instead of accidental."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When an idea is ready to become real work"
  record="Write the steps you take from idea to defined work bundle">
  How do I go from a project idea to a work bundle? How can I refine my ideas / learning objectives into projects?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Completed projects are evidence of which objectives I've actually advanced. Reflecting backward closes the loop, showing me whether my finished work served the goals I claimed to care about."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When reviewing what finished projects actually moved forward"
  record="For each completed project, note which objectives it advanced">
  What objectives were advanced by the projects I have already completed? What objectives have I advanced through my projects?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
