There is always more to learn than time to learn it. The hard part is not finding material, it is deciding what deserves my attention next and then actually committing to it. These questions walk from picking topics, to prioritizing them, to building a curriculum, to protecting the time and keeping the commitments.

<!-- truncate -->

## What to learn

<SectionBanner why="The first step is surfacing everything I might want to learn before judging any of it. Casting a wide net here keeps me from defaulting to whatever's trendy or in front of me." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Before I can prioritize, I have to name the field. Listing what I want to learn and what I should be learning are two different lists, and the gap between them is worth seeing."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When deciding where learning time should go"
  record="Two lists: what you want to learn, and what you should">
  What do I want to learn? What should I be learning?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Mastery is different from dabbling, and it has to be chosen on purpose. Asking what's valuable to master, and where my strengths and weaknesses lie, points learning at what actually compounds."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When deciding what to go deep on versus sample"
  record="Name what's worth mastering; note your real strengths and weaknesses">
  What should I master in, and what is valuable for me to learn? What are my strengths and weaknesses?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="What I want to accomplish in life implies what I need to learn to get there. Working backward from the life goal to the learning, and naming where I want to grow, keeps study tied to something that matters."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When connecting learning to longer-term goals"
  record="Write the life goal, then the learning it implies, then where you want to grow">
  What do I want to accomplish in my life, and what learning does that imply? Where do I want to grow?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Learning often comes in bundles, not single topics, and some of it is side interest rather than core. A learning catalogue captures all of it in one place so nothing gets lost."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When organizing the full range of things I want to learn"
  record="Build a catalogue of bundles, including the side interests">
  What bundles of knowledge do I want to learn more about? What do I want to study on the side? Should I make a learning catalogue of everything I want to learn?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Learning priorities

<SectionBanner why="With the field surfaced, the next job is ordering it. Difficulty estimates and value tell me what to learn first, so I'm not spending my best energy on the least useful thing." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Priority and difficulty together tell me where to start. Knowing which topics I've done before lets me estimate effort, and naming what's most valuable to learn first keeps me from sequencing badly."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When ordering the learning backlog"
  record="Rank topics by value; flag the ones you've done before as easier to estimate">
  What are my learning priorities? Which topics have I done before, so I can estimate their level of difficulty? What is most valuable for me to learn first?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Your learning plan and curriculum

<SectionBanner why="A plan turns intentions into a path: what I'll learn, by when, and in what order. This section builds the curriculum and the backlog so learning has structure instead of being a pile of bookmarks." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="A learning plan with dates and phases is what turns a wish list into a curriculum. Naming what I'll learn, by when, and in what sequence makes the journey real instead of perpetual someday-learning."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When designing or revising the curriculum"
  record="Write the plan: topics, target dates, and the phases in order">
  What is my learning plan? What will I learn, and by when? What is my learning journey and curriculum? What will the phases of my learning be?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Goals make learning measurable, and choosing what to focus on now keeps the plan from being everything at once. This is where the broad field gets narrowed to a workable focus."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When setting concrete learning goals for the season"
  record="Write the learning goals and the one focus for now">
  What learning goals will I set for myself? What do I think is worth learning, and what should I focus on now?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A backlog holds everything I could learn; a commitment marks the slice I've actually signed up for. Keeping both, and tracking which backlog items I've committed to, keeps the plan honest about capacity."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When promoting items from backlog to committed"
  record="Maintain a backlog; mark which items you've actually committed to learn">
  What is my learning backlog of things I could possibly learn? Which items from my backlog have I actually committed myself to learning?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Making time for learning

<SectionBanner why="A plan is worthless without protected time, and learning time is the first thing the day eats. This section is about carving out and reclaiming hours, including the idle ones." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Learning loses to everything urgent unless its time is explicitly protected. Asking how to maximize and explicitly allocate learning time is what keeps it from being perpetually squeezed out."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When planning the week and protecting learning blocks"
  record="Write the hours you've explicitly blocked for learning">
  How do I maximize how much time I allocate to learning? What time have I explicitly allocated for learning?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="quick"
  why="Idle time, exercising, vacuuming, doing dishes, grilling, is hidden learning capacity if the material fits the moment. Figuring out what kind of material sticks during those windows unlocks hours I'm already spending."
  howOften="As situations come up"
  when="When looking for hidden pockets of time to learn"
  record="Note which material actually sticks during hands-busy time">
  Could I learn during idle time, the way some people listen to audiobooks while exercising, vacuuming, doing the dishes, or grilling? Can I learn from any material while exercising, or is there a specific kind that sticks better?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Learning commitments

<SectionBanner why="A commitment is what turns a plan into something I'm accountable to. This section pins down exactly what I've signed up to learn, how much time per week, and whether I'm keeping up." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Vague intent to 'learn more' never survives a busy week. Naming exactly what I've committed to, the resources I'll work through, and a concrete weekly number like four hours of studying, reading, and programming, is what makes it stick. Checking whether I'm keeping up keeps the commitment honest."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When making or reviewing a learning commitment"
  record="Write the specific commitment, the resources, the weekly hours, and whether you're on track">
  How will I commit myself to learning, and what exactly have I committed to learn? What resources have I committed to working through? How much time am I going to spend learning every week? Could I aim for something like 4 hours per week of studying, reading, and programming? Am I keeping up with my commitments?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Certifications are one milestone, but not the only one. Asking why they matter and whether they're the only proof of learning keeps me from chasing credentials when real understanding is the actual goal."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When deciding whether a certification is worth pursuing"
  record="Note which certifications matter and what other milestones prove your learning">
  Should I be working on any certifications? Why are certifications important? Are certifications the only milestone that can reflect my level of learning?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Overlapping learning with work

<SectionBanner why="The cheapest learning is the kind that overlaps with what I'm already building. This section looks for where work and study can reinforce each other instead of competing for hours." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Work hands me chances to learn things on someone else's clock. Adjusting my plan around the technologies I'll touch at work, and deliberately overlapping learning with building, doubles the return on both."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When work introduces a technology I want to learn anyway"
  record="Note where what you're learning can overlap with what you're building">
  How might my learning plan shift based on the opportunities I have to work on different technologies at work? Where can I deliberately overlap what I am learning with what I am building?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Consolidating your learning material

<SectionBanner why="Learning material scattered across bookmarks, links, photos, and screenshots is material I'll never actually use. This section is about gathering it all into one place I can return to." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Material spread across browser bookmarks, LinkedIn saves, phone photos of books, and screenshots might as well not exist. Consolidating and indexing it in one place is what makes it usable instead of just collected."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When learning material has scattered across too many places"
  record="Pick one index; pull bookmarks, saved links, book photos, and screenshots into it">
  Where do I capture notes on all the different things I want to learn from? How do I index all of my learning material in one place? Could I categorize my browser bookmarks, consolidate saved links from LinkedIn, gather the photos of books on my phone, and collect screenshots of useful websites?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
