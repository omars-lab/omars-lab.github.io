Priorities drift quietly. What mattered last month keeps occupying my calendar long after it stopped being the point. These are the questions I return to when I want to step back and check whether my time and energy are still flowing toward what actually matters. I read them slowly, and let the uncomfortable ones surface what's been on autopilot.

<!-- truncate -->

## Current priorities

<SectionBanner why="Naming what matters right now is the anchor for everything else. If I can't say my top priorities out loud, my calendar will fill itself with whatever shouts loudest." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="If I can't name my current priorities clearly, I have no standard to measure my time against. This is the baseline question that the rest depend on."
  howOften="Weekly, as part of a planning ritual"
  when="At the start of a week, or whenever I feel scattered"
  record="Write the top three. If the list runs longer than five, it isn't a priority list yet.">
  What are your top priorities right now? What is currently important to you?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Commitment is the difference between a wish and a priority. Naming what I've actually committed my time to exposes the gap between what I say matters and what I'm spending hours on."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When reviewing how the past month actually went"
  record="List the goals you've committed real time to this month">
  What goals are you committing yourself and your time to?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Growth doesn't happen by accident. Naming the specific areas I've chosen keeps me from spreading thin across too many fronts at once."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When deciding where to invest discretionary energy"
  record="Pick one or two areas of growth, not ten">
  What areas of growth have you prioritized?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Time allocation

<SectionBanner why="A priority you don't fund with time is just a preference. This section checks whether my hours line up with what I claim matters, and forces the today-versus-eventually split." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="This is the accountability question. It compares the ideal allocation of my time against the real one, and the gap is usually uncomfortable, which is exactly why it's useful."
  howOften="Weekly. Check it against the calendar you actually lived"
  when="At a weekly review, looking back at how the hours went"
  record="Write where time should go, then where it actually went; the delta is the work">
  How should you be spending your time? Are you spending it on what matters most?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Sorting now from eventually keeps urgent-but-trivial things from crowding out important-but-quiet ones. Without the split, everything feels equally pressing."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When planning the week or triaging a task list"
  record="Two columns: do immediately, do eventually">
  What should you immediately do? What do you need to do eventually?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="daily"
  depth="quick"
  why="Tomorrow is built by what I do today. This question forces me to connect a daily action to a future I actually want, instead of just reacting to the day."
  howOften="Daily"
  when="At the start of the day, when setting the day's focus"
  record="Name one thing today that makes tomorrow easier">
  What needs to be done today to enable tomorrow?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Priority shifts

<SectionBanner why="Priorities change, and pretending they don't is how stale goals keep eating my time. This section makes the changes explicit so I can act on them instead of drifting." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The 'why' is the part that keeps a priority honest. If I can't explain why something is top priority right now, it probably isn't, or I've stopped questioning it."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="During a monthly review of goals"
  record="For each top goal, write the reason it earns that rank today">
  Which of your goals are top priority right now, and why?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Naming what dropped is as important as naming what rose. A goal that quietly fell off deserves an honest reason, otherwise I'll feel guilty about abandoning it without ever deciding to."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When something that used to matter has gone quiet"
  record="Write what dropped and the honest reason it did">
  Which of your goals dropped in priority, and why?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The goals I'm not tracking are the ones most likely to be neglected. This question surfaces the important things that have fallen outside any system."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="During a broader review of where my attention actually goes"
  record="List anything important that lives only in your head, not in a system">
  Are there any important goals in your life that you're not tracking?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Ignoring a goal is a decision, even when it's made by default. Naming the ignored ones lets me choose them on purpose or drop them on purpose."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When reviewing the full set of goals"
  record="List the goals you've been avoiding, then decide: commit or drop">
  What goals are you ignoring?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Task prioritization

<SectionBanner why="Not all tasks are equal, and the ones that pay off aren't always the ones I want to do. This section sorts the high-value work from the merely appealing." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Benefit and desire often point in different directions. Seeing both for the same task list tells me where I need discipline and where I can just follow my energy."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When choosing what to actually work on next"
  record="Mark each task: high-benefit, want-to-do, or both">
  What tasks will benefit you the most? What tasks do you actually want to do?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Prioritization is a skill, not a feeling. Having an actual method beats sorting by whatever feels most urgent in the moment."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When the task list has grown faster than the time to do it"
  record="Write down the rule you use to rank, so it's repeatable">
  How will you properly prioritize what you should be working on or thinking about?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Ignored tasks accumulate quietly until they become a crisis. A regular check keeps the backlog from rotting out of sight."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="During a weekly task review"
  record="Scan for stalled tasks; note which ones you've been keeping up with">
  Are you ignoring any tasks? Are you keeping up with them?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Learning priorities

<SectionBanner why="Learning is a priority that's easy to defer because nothing breaks when I skip it. Naming what I should be learning keeps growth from being the thing that always gets cut." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="There's always more to learn than time to learn it. Naming what I should be learning, specifically, keeps me from drifting toward whatever's easiest or most entertaining."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When planning where to spend learning time"
  record="List the topics that would most move your goals forward">
  What are the things you should be learning?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="A reading list is a learning plan in disguise. Choosing the books on purpose beats grabbing whatever happens to be in front of me."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When finishing a book and deciding what's next"
  record="Keep a short, intentional reading queue">
  What are the books you should be reading?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Some learning is for the job; some is for the mind. Naming what expands my thinking, not just my skills, keeps me from only ever learning the immediately useful."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="When my thinking feels narrow or stuck in one mode"
  record="Name the learning that stretches how you think, not just what you can do">
  What do you need to learn to expand your mind?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Goals and plans

<SectionBanner why="A goal without a plan stays a hope. This section pushes each goal toward the concrete: the projects, the timing, and the reasons behind them." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Goals and plans are different things. Goals say where I'm going; plans say how. Confusing the two is how people end up with ambitions they never act on."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="At the start of a new quarter or major chapter"
  record="Write the goals, then the plan for each; keep them visibly separate">
  Have you figured out your goals? What are your plans?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Projects are where goals become work. Sorting what needs to finish soon from what I should pick up next keeps the in-progress pile from quietly growing forever."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When reviewing active and upcoming projects"
  record="Two lists: finish soon, and start next">
  What projects do you need to finish soon? What projects should you work on?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The 'why' behind a project is what keeps me going when it gets hard. Choosing the kind of project on purpose, with a reason, beats taking on whatever lands in my lap."
  howOften="Monthly"
  when="When deciding whether to take on a new project"
  record="For each candidate project, write the reason it's worth your time">
  What kind of projects should you be working on, and why?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="A goal without a reason and a date is just a label. Attaching the why and the when turns intention into something I can actually schedule."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="When committing to an action"
  record="For each commitment, note the reason and the date">
  Why and when are you going to do something?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Weekly planning

<SectionBanner why="The weekly ritual is the heartbeat of staying on track. Without a repeatable rhythm, planning happens only in crisis, which is too late to matter." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="A ritual is what makes planning reliable instead of occasional. If I can't describe my weekly ritual, I probably don't have one, and the week runs me instead."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="At the same anchor point each week"
  record="Write down the steps of your weekly ritual so it's repeatable">
  What is your weekly planning ritual?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="The week is the unit where intention meets reality. Naming this week's goals turns a vague sense of busyness into a small, checkable list."
  howOften="Weekly"
  when="At the start of the week"
  record="Pick a handful of goals for the week, no more">
  What are this week's goals?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Quests and dreams

<SectionBanner why="Beneath the weekly tasks are the bigger things I'm actually chasing. To achieve my goals and my dreams, I set out on quests, and these questions keep those quests in view." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Framing goals as quests changes how I relate to them: a quest is something I commit to and strive for, not just a box to tick. This question keeps the bigger pursuits alive behind the daily list."
  howOften="Quarterly"
  when="During a longer reflection on direction"
  record="Name the quests you're on and what each is really for">
  What quests are you on? What goals are you chasing? What will you strive to do?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Dreams stay dreams until they have a path. This question forces me to turn the thing I want most into a sequence of steps I could actually take."
  howOften="Quarterly, and whenever a dream feels stalled"
  when="When a dream has lived in my head long enough to need a plan"
  record="Write the dream, then the first concrete step toward realizing it">
  What do you need to do to realize your dreams? How will you realize them?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="yearly"
  depth="deep"
  why="What I want to become and master is the deepest layer of priority. It's the slow-moving aim that all the weekly work is supposed to serve."
  howOften="Yearly"
  when="During annual reflection on identity and direction"
  record="Write what you want to become and what you want to master; revisit it across years">
  What do you want to become? What do you want to master?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
