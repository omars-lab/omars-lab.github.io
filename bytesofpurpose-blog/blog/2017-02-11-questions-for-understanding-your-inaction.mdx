Knowing what I want is easy. Knowing why I still haven't done it is the harder, more
useful work. These are the questions I return to when I'm stuck in a loop, when I keep
choosing the logical thing over the meaningful one, or when something I clearly want
just isn't getting done. I read them slowly and answer the ones that make me flinch.

## Naming the inaction

<SectionBanner why="I can't fix a problem I haven't named. Before any of the why or how, I have to admit plainly what I'm not doing and stop letting it stay vague enough to ignore." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="core"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Naming the specific thing I'm not doing, but should be, drags it out of the fog where it's easy to avoid. The two angles, what I want to do and what I should be doing, catch both the longing and the obligation I'm dodging."
  howOften="Monthly, and any time I feel a low hum of avoidance"
  when="When I sense I'm dodging something but haven't made myself say what"
  record="Write the specific undone thing in plain words; vagueness is how it survives">
  What haven't you done that you want to do? What are you not doing that you should be doing?
</Question>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'chisel']}
  priority="core"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="deep"
  why="The repeated 'why' is the point: the first answer is usually an excuse, and the truth sits a few layers down. Asking it from several angles wears down the surface story until the real reason shows."
  howOften="Monthly, whenever I notice the same thing staying undone"
  when="When I've named the inaction and now need the honest reason for it"
  record="Write the first answer, then keep asking 'and why that' until I hit something I can't dodge">
  Why have you not done it yet? Why are you not doing it? Why don't you do it?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="quick"
  why="'When will you start' converts reflection into a commitment with a date. Without a when, understanding my inaction just becomes a more sophisticated way of continuing it."
  howOften="Whenever I've understood the why and am tempted to stop there"
  when="The moment I catch myself analyzing instead of starting"
  record="Write an actual date or trigger; 'someday' doesn't count">
  When will you start?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Barriers preventing action

<SectionBanner why="Some of what stops me is real and removable; some of it is imaginary. These questions sort the genuine barriers from the excuses and ask what would actually have to change for me to move." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Naming the barriers turns a vague stuckness into a list of specific obstacles, and a list can be worked through one item at a time. What feels like an immovable wall is usually a few separate, smaller things."
  howOften="Monthly, when something stays blocked"
  when="When I feel generally stuck and need to break the block into parts"
  record="List each barrier separately; a single 'I can't' usually hides three or four real ones">
  What barriers are preventing action?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Asking what support or resources I'd need shifts me from 'I can't' to 'here's what would make it possible.' It reframes the obstacle as a gap to fill rather than a verdict on whether I can act."
  howOften="When I've named a barrier and want a path around it"
  when="When I'm blocked by a lack of something rather than a lack of will"
  record="List the specific support or resources needed, and who or what could provide each">
  What support or resources would you need to move forward?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="'What would need to change' makes my conditions for action explicit, and often reveals that I'm waiting on something I could change myself. The conditions I'm waiting on are usually fewer and more in my control than they feel."
  howOften="When I catch myself waiting for conditions to be right"
  when="When I keep saying 'once X happens, then I'll act'"
  record="Write the changes I'm waiting on, then mark which I could actually trigger myself">
  What would need to change for you to take action?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="This is the question that turns a list of barriers into progress, because not every barrier is real or removable, but some are. Finding the one I can actually clear today is how the loop finally breaks."
  howOften="Right after listing my barriers"
  when="When I have a barrier list and need to find the lever that actually moves"
  record="Circle the removable barriers; pick the one I can clear first and start there">
  Which of these barriers can actually be removed?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Patterns that keep you stuck

<SectionBanner why="My inaction usually isn't a one-off; it's a pattern I repeat. These questions look for the loop itself, including the trap of choosing the safe, logical thing over the meaningful one, and the comfort I've found in staying stuck." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'anvil']}
  priority="core"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="A single instance of inaction is a moment; a pattern is a character trait in the making. Finding the recurring shape of how I get stuck is worth far more than solving any one stuck thing, because the pattern will produce the next one."
  howOften="Quarterly, looking back across several stuck moments"
  when="When I notice I've been stuck the same way more than once"
  record="Write the common thread across my stuck moments; the repeated element is the real target">
  What patterns keep you from taking action?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Being stuck in a loop and being 'too logical' are linked: the logical choice is often the safe one that quietly avoids the meaningful, riskier thing. This question names the trap of reasoning my way out of every leap worth taking."
  howOften="Monthly, when I notice repetitive safe choices"
  when="When I keep picking the sensible option over the one that actually matters to me"
  record="Note where I chose logical-and-safe over meaningful-and-risky; ask what the logic was protecting me from">
  Are you stuck in a loop? Are you doing things that are too logical?
</Question>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'chisel']}
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Comfort in the darkness is the most dangerous kind of stuckness, because it stops feeling like a problem. This asks why I've made peace with a place I shouldn't stay, which is usually the first honest step out of it."
  howOften="Quarterly, and whenever a bad situation has started to feel normal"
  when="When something painful has quietly become my comfort zone"
  record="Write what the darkness gives me that I'm afraid to lose; that's the hook keeping me there">
  Why have you gotten comfortable in the darkness?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="quick"
  why="Flipping the question to 'why NOT do things' attacks the inertia from the other side. Often there's no good reason not to act, and forcing myself to defend the inaction exposes how thin its justification really is."
  howOften="Whenever I'm defaulting to not acting"
  when="When my reason for not acting is really just inertia in disguise"
  record="Try to make the honest case for not doing it; if I can't, that's my answer">
  Why NOT do things?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Constraints, self-imposed and external

<SectionBanner why="Some of my limits I built myself and some came from others, and they call for different responses. These questions separate the two and surface the needs and emotions quietly running the whole pattern of inaction." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="The constraints I imposed on myself are the ones I can lift the moment I see them clearly. They're often invisible precisely because they're mine, so naming them is most of the work of removing them."
  howOften="Quarterly, when I feel hemmed in"
  when="When I catch myself treating a self-made rule as if it were a law of nature"
  record="List my self-imposed constraints; for each, ask whether I'd keep it if I were choosing fresh today">
  What constraints have you imposed on yourself?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Constraints others put on me are real, but I often grant them more power than they actually hold. Separating these from my self-imposed ones tells me which limits to negotiate and which to simply stop obeying."
  howOften="Quarterly, alongside the self-imposed check"
  when="When I'm not sure whether a limit is truly external or one I've adopted"
  record="List the external constraints, then mark which are genuinely binding and which I've just accepted">
  What constraints have others imposed on you?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Underneath most of my inaction is a need, belief, or emotion quietly steering me, often a fear I haven't named. Surfacing what's really controlling me is how I stop being run by it and start acting on purpose."
  howOften="Quarterly, and any time my inaction defies my own logic"
  when="When I want something, know how to get it, and still don't move"
  record="Write the honest needs, beliefs, and emotions driving the stall, including the ones I'd rather not admit">
  What are the needs, beliefs, and emotions that are controlling you?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
