When people talk about "learning," they usually mean one or two channels: a book,
maybe a course. But the actual menu is much wider, and most of the leverage comes
from knowing the whole menu so you can pick the right channel for the thing you're
trying to learn. This is the taxonomy I keep, with the questions I ask before
committing time to any of it.

<!-- truncate -->

The cards under each category are a living list of the specific resources I've
collected. They grow over time. If a category has no cards yet, I simply haven't
filled it in.

## Getting started

If you just want a place to begin, you don't need the whole menu. Start here:

[Step 1] Pick **one book** on the thing you're learning, and write down why you're
reading it and what you expect to get out of it.

[Step 2] Find **one person** who's further along (a mentor, or someone whose work
you admire) and study how they learned it.

[Step 3] Keep a **running list of videos and blogs** for the in-between time, so idle
moments turn into progress instead of hunting.

[Step 4] Once you're moving, **reach for the rest of the menu** below as the work
demands it: papers when you hit the edge, repositories to read real code,
competitions when you need pressure and a goal.

The rest of this post is that full menu.

## Why a taxonomy at all

A resource type is worthless until you can answer two questions about it: *why am I
reaching for this one*, and *what do I expect to get out of it*. A taxonomy makes
those questions concrete. Instead of "I should learn more," you get "this is a
*mentors* problem, not a *books* problem," which is a far more actionable place to
start.

## The channels

### Books

The default, and still one of the best. The useful discipline is to be deliberate:
why am I reading *this* book, what do I expect to learn from it, and how will I find
the next one? Reading lists from people you respect are a strong source. Libraries,
ebook memberships, and bundle sites keep the cost down, and audiobooks open up time
you'd otherwise lose.

### Courses

Structured, paced, and good when you need a guided path rather than a reference.
The questions: which courses am I actually taking versus collecting, which do I
*need* versus merely want, and when will I take them? The trap with courses is
accumulation. A subscription you never open is not learning.

<CategoryCarousel topic="learning-resources" category="courses" />

### Videos

Best for the times when reading isn't an option (on a treadmill, commuting, winding
down). The practice that makes videos work is curation: keep a running, organized
list of what to watch and where you get it from, so the moment you have idle time
you're not hunting for something.

<CategoryCarousel topic="learning-resources" category="videos" />

### Blogs

Where you find the current, opinionated, in-the-weeds thinking that hasn't made it
into books yet. Worth maintaining a small set you follow, and treating an
interesting author as a node in a network: who do *they* read and link to?

<CategoryCarousel topic="learning-resources" category="blogs" />

### Mentors

The highest-bandwidth channel, and the one people underuse. The questions are about
*what* a mentor is for: refining your learning plan, pointing you at what matters
more than what's merely interesting, and naming the gaps you can't see in yourself.
Choosing one is its own filter: who do I respect, who has the bandwidth, and would
they be willing to review the actual work I produce?

<CategoryCarousel topic="learning-resources" category="mentors" />

### Pioneers, masters, and experts

Even without direct access, you can learn from the field's big names by studying
what they wrote, recorded, and built. Pick a few people whose work you admire and
go deep on their output.

<CategoryCarousel topic="learning-resources" category="experts" />

### Research papers

Where the field's edge actually lives. The hard part is access and navigation:
where do practitioners look for papers, who can point you at the good ones, and how
do you use a paper's references to find the rest of the conversation.

### Everything else

Beyond those, the menu keeps going, and each entry is the right tool for a specific
job:

- **Conferences** for talks, presentations, and the pulse of an industry.
- **Forums** (Stack Overflow, Reddit, specialized communities) for problem-shaped,
  just-in-time answers.
- **Repositories** for learning by reading real open-source code.
- **Cheatsheets** for fast reference once you already understand the thing.
- **Curriculums** for a structured path, including published university curricula.
- **Competitions** (Kaggle, hackathons, coding challenges) for learning under
  pressure with a clear goal.
- **Standards and specifications** for the authoritative definition of how
  something is supposed to work.
- **Reports** for a synthesized industry-level view.
- **Presentations** for a compressed, opinionated take on a topic.
- **Platforms** that bundle many of the above into one place.
- **Datasets** for the kind of learning that only happens by doing.

## Maintaining the list

The taxonomy is only useful if it's alive. The maintenance questions are simple but
worth asking on a rhythm: what can I actually learn from right now, and which
resources am I leaning on most? The ones you reach for repeatedly are signal. Prune
the rest, and keep refilling the channels you've found pay off.
