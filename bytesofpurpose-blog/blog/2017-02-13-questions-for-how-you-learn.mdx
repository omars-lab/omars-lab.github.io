Most of us were taught *what* to learn, but rarely *how* we ourselves learn best. Getting deliberate about my own process changes everything: how I find a topic, how I absorb it, and how I know when I've actually got it. The questions below walk through learning style, process, seeking knowledge, expanding it, the methods worth studying, and how to keep the whole thing in motion.

## Your learning style

<SectionBanner why="I learn differently than the next person, and naming how I actually take things in lets me lean into it instead of fighting it." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question

  cron="adhoc"

## Your learning process

<SectionBanner why="A learning style is how I take things in; a process is the repeatable sequence I run so I'm always learning something on purpose. The bullets below are a starting shape for mine." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Learning decays if I leave it to chance. A defined process is what keeps me in a state of learning instead of drifting in and out of it."
  howOften="Monthly, as a check that the process is still running"
  when="During a monthly review of how I've been spending my attention"
  record="Document my process as steps, and revise it when a step stops earning its place">
  What do I need to continually do to ensure that I am always in a state of learning? What does my personal learning process look like?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

A starting shape for that process:

- Capture a whole bunch of questions that intrigue me around a topic
- Formulate a backlog to learn more about the topic
- Come up with fun projects that may overlap with learning about one or more topics
- As needed, come up with a plan for longer backlogs
- Capture repetitive tasks

## Seeking knowledge effectively

<SectionBanner why="A guiding principle for this section: employ the scientific method as much as possible. Treat curiosity as a hypothesis to test, not just a feeling to follow." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="Hunger is a signal. If I can trace it to a real intellectual or spiritual gap, I learn the right thing instead of chasing whatever is shiny."
  howOften="When a strong urge to learn something shows up, before acting on it"
  when="At the moment I feel pulled toward a new topic"
  record="Write the gap the hunger points to (intellectual, spiritual), then decide if it's worth filling now">
  Why do I hunger for a certain piece of knowledge? (Identify my intellectual gaps and my spiritual gaps.)
</Question>

<Question
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Studying the big names in a field is the fastest way to inherit its hard-won structure instead of rediscovering it slowly on my own."
  howOften="At the start of any serious dive into a new field"
  when="Right after I've chosen a topic and before I start consuming randomly"
  record="List the key figures, then collect what they wrote and worked on">
  After figuring out a topic I wish to learn more about, who are the big names in the field? (Find books they wrote, projects in the same context, lectures and videos to watch.)
</Question>

<Question
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="What I do while observing decides what I retain. Notes, structure, and graphs turn passive consumption into something I can actually use later."
  howOften="Every time I sit down to study something"
  when="While reading, listening, or watching"
  record="Take notes, structure them, make graphs, and learn the setup even when I don't like it">
  While observing, how do I capture what I'm taking in? (Take notes and document ideas, structure them into graphs, and learn the setup even if I don't like it.)
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Expanding your knowledge

<SectionBanner why="Seeking knowledge gets me started; expanding it is the deliberate work of mapping, classifying, and connecting what I take in so it compounds. The list below is the sequence I run." />

A sequence for expanding what I know:

1. Map what I know and what I don't
2. Identify all the areas of research that overlap with my interests
3. Classify all the books I run into
4. Figure out what I intend to read and for what purpose
5. Identify an area in which I wish to grow and identify the books that will best help me achieve that goal
6. Take notes while reading
7. Expand the concepts I am aware of
8. Track all the books I've read, and mark the pages that stand out

## Learning methods

<SectionBanner why="There's more than one way to learn, and knowing the named methods lets me pick the right tool instead of always defaulting to 'read more.'" />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="quick"
  why="If I only know one way to learn, I'll force every topic through it. Naming alternatives gives me options for the ones that don't fit."
  howOften="When a topic isn't sticking with my usual approach"
  when="Before starting a subject that feels unfamiliar in shape"
  record="Keep a list of learning methods and which kinds of material each suits">
  What are different ways in which I can learn?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

Topics worth learning more about:

- Socratic Method of Learning (ask questions and find answers)
- Instructional Design (crafting courseware to maximize how people learn)
- Bloom's Taxonomy: kinesthetic (do), knowledge (know), affective (feel)
- "Education is to understand complexity; training is to understand function"

## Maintaining a learning mindset

<SectionBanner why="The hardest part isn't starting to learn, it's not stopping. These questions check whether I'm still a sponge or have quietly gone stale." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A learning mindset is a habit that erodes silently. Checking on it regularly catches the drift before it becomes a rut."
  howOften="Weekly, as part of a lightweight reflection"
  when="During a weekly review, when assessing my energy and curiosity"
  record="Note whether I learned anything new this week, and what dulled my curiosity if I didn't">
  How do I stay in a state of continuous learning? Am I always being a sponge, making sure to never stop learning?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Boredom is data. The point where I get bored is usually the point where my learning method stopped fitting the material."
  howOften="Whenever I notice I've checked out of something I was learning"
  when="When my attention on a topic starts to fade"
  record="Write down what was happening right before I lost interest">
  When do I get bored, and when do I stop learning?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Self-teaching vs needing a teacher

<SectionBanner why="Some things I can figure out alone; others need a guide. Knowing which is which keeps me from either struggling needlessly or outsourcing what I should own." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A teacher is leverage, but also a dependency. Drawing the line tells me where to invest in help and where to build self-reliance."
  howOften="When deciding how to approach a new, difficult subject"
  when="Before committing to either self-study or a course"
  record="Note which topics I genuinely need a teacher for and why">
  What do I need a teacher to learn versus what can I learn on my own? Why do I need a teacher?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Capturing your learnings

<SectionBanner why="Knowledge I don't capture leaks away. These questions are about building the storehouse so what I learn stays findable and reusable." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="If I can't find what I learned, I didn't really keep it. A capture-and-organize habit is what makes learning compound over years."
  howOften="Weekly, to keep the capture habit alive"
  when="After finishing a study session or a project"
  record="A note system where each learning has a home, organized so I can retrieve it">
  How do I capture my learnings, and where? How will I organize and keep track of what I want to learn?
</Question>

<Question
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Taking stock of what I've already learned and noted keeps me from relearning the same things and shows me how far I've come."
  howOften="Monthly, as a review of accumulated notes"
  when="During a monthly reflection on progress"
  record="Review my captured notes and the gotchas I've collected">
  What have I already learned? What notes have I captured? What gotchas have I encountered?
</Question>

<Question
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="quick"
  why="Diagrams force me to structure understanding, and they're far faster to revisit later than a wall of prose."
  howOften="When a learning is complex enough that a picture would help"
  when="While capturing something with many moving parts"
  record="Create the diagram and store it alongside the related notes">
  What diagrams will I create to capture my learnings?
</Question>

<Question
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="quick"
  why="Tool and component knowledge is easy to lose between projects. A dedicated home for it saves me from rediscovering the same setup details."
  howOften="Whenever I learn something useful about a tool or component"
  when="Right after wrestling with a tool's quirks"
  record="A place to log lessons per tool and per software component">
  Where do I capture the things I learned about the different tools I have used, and about different software components?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
