Most of us make decisions and never look back at them. But the decisions already
behind you are the cheapest teacher you'll ever have. These are the questions I
return to when I want to understand my own patterns, separate good reasoning from
lucky outcomes, and get a little better at the next call. Pick the ones that make
you pause.

<!-- truncate -->

## Reviewing past decisions

<SectionBanner why="The decisions already behind you are the cheapest data you have. Looking back deliberately is what turns a finished choice into a lesson." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Big decisions slip by unexamined unless you make a point of naming them. Listing the recent ones is the raw material for every other question here."
  howOften="Monthly, as a quick inventory of what you've decided"
  when="During a monthly review or at the close of a busy stretch"
  record="Keep a running list of significant decisions as you make them">
  What major decisions have you made recently?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Pride and regret are both signals about your values. Sorting decisions by how you feel about them reveals what you actually care about getting right."
  howOften="Quarterly, looking back across a longer arc"
  when="During a seasonal review or after a chapter closes"
  record="Two columns: proud of, would redo. Note the reason in each row">
  What decisions are you most proud of? Which do you wish you could redo?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A handful of decisions shape most of a life. Knowing which ones carried the most weight teaches you where to slow down next time."
  howOften="Quarterly, or during longer life reviews"
  when="When reflecting on how you got to where you are"
  record="Rank the highest-impact decisions and note what made each pivotal">
  Which decisions had the biggest impact on your life?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Understanding outcomes

<SectionBanner why="A decision and its outcome are two different things. Studying how a call actually played out is how you keep your expectations honest." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Comparing what happened to what you expected is the calibration loop. Without it, your predictions never get more accurate."
  howOften="After a decision has had time to play out"
  when="Once the results of a choice are visible enough to judge"
  record="Write your original expectation next to what actually happened">
  How did that decision turn out? Did reality match your expectations?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="What surprised you marks the edge of your model of the world. Surprises are where the real learning is hiding."
  howOften="After any outcome that didn't go how you pictured"
  when="When a result lands differently than you assumed it would"
  record="Note the specific surprise and what assumption it broke">
  What surprised you about the outcome?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Confusing a good outcome with a good decision is how you learn the wrong lesson. Separating skill from luck protects you from rewarding bad reasoning that happened to pay off."
  howOften="After any notable win or loss, before you draw conclusions"
  when="When you're tempted to credit yourself, or blame yourself, for a result"
  record="Judge the decision on what you knew at the time, not on how it ended">
  Was the outcome due to good decision-making or luck?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Learning from decisions

<SectionBanner why="A decision you don't learn from is one you'll likely repeat. These questions extract the lesson while the experience is still warm." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="high"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The lesson is the entire point of looking back. Naming what you'd do differently is what makes the next decision better instead of identical."
  howOften="Monthly, and after any decision worth examining"
  when="Once you can see how a choice turned out"
  record="Write one concrete thing you'd change and one you'd keep">
  What did you learn from this decision? What would you do differently next time?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The gap between what you knew and what you needed to know is where better decisions come from. Naming the missing information teaches you what to gather next time."
  howOften="After a decision, especially one that went sideways"
  when="When reviewing a call that depended on incomplete information"
  record="List what you had and what you wish you'd had">
  What information did you have, and what were you missing?
</Question>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="Good reasoning can lead to bad results, and bad reasoning can get lucky. Judging the process apart from the outcome is the single most important habit for deciding well over time."
  howOften="After meaningful decisions, before you update your approach"
  when="When a result tempts you to abandon a process that was actually sound"
  record="Write whether the reasoning held up, independent of how it ended">
  Was your reasoning sound even if the outcome wasn't?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Decision patterns

<SectionBanner why="Your worst decisions tend to rhyme. Spotting the recurring shape of how you decide is how you stop repeating the same mistake in new clothes." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Patterns are where the leverage is. One fix to a recurring tendency improves every future decision that shares the shape."
  howOften="Quarterly, looking across many decisions at once"
  when="When you have enough decisions logged to see the through-lines"
  record="Name the patterns you keep repeating, good and bad">
  What patterns do you see in your decision-making?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Speed is a bias too. Knowing whether you rush or stall tells you which correction to apply before the next big call."
  howOften="Quarterly, as you review your decision patterns"
  when="When you notice a decision went wrong on timing rather than substance"
  record="Note your default tempo and where it has cost you">
  Do you tend to decide too quickly or too slowly?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Everyone leans on one faculty too hard. Naming your tilt and your biases is how you stop being quietly steered by them."
  howOften="Quarterly, and when a decision feels lopsided"
  when="When you catch yourself defending a choice on feel alone, or on logic alone"
  record="List the biases you recognize in yourself and where they show up">
  Do you over-rely on logic or emotion? What biases affect your decisions?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="You decide better under some conditions than others. Knowing when you're at your best lets you schedule important calls for those windows."
  howOften="Quarterly, mapping your conditions against your outcomes"
  when="When reviewing where your strongest and weakest calls came from"
  record="Note the conditions present during your best and worst decisions">
  When do you make your best decisions? Your worst?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Processing regrets

<SectionBanner why="Regret is information about what mattered to you, but it can also become a weight you carry past its usefulness. These questions mine the lesson and then let the rest go." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="Regretting a bad process is fair; regretting a bad outcome you couldn't have foreseen is just self-punishment. Separating the two tells you what to actually fix."
  howOften="When a regret keeps surfacing and won't settle"
  when="When you keep replaying a decision and can't tell why it stings"
  record="Write whether you regret how you decided or just how it ended">
  What decisions do you regret? Is it the process or the outcome you regret?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A regret you learn from converts a loss into tuition. The lesson is the only part worth keeping."
  howOften="When processing a regret, after the sting has cooled"
  when="Once you can look at a painful decision without flinching"
  record="Write the single lesson the regret is trying to teach you">
  What can you learn from these regrets?
</Question>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'anvil']}
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="Some decisions can't be reversed, and carrying them forever changes nothing but you. Making peace is what frees the energy that regret was eating."
  howOften="When an irreversible decision keeps draining you"
  when="When you've extracted the lesson but still can't set the decision down"
  record="Write what you've learned, then write your permission to release the rest">
  How do you make peace with decisions you can't undo?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Regret past its expiration is dead weight. Asking whether it still serves you is how you decide to put it down."
  howOften="When you notice an old regret still pulling at you"
  when="When a regret has stopped teaching and started just hurting"
  record="A yes or no, plus what you'd reclaim by letting it go">
  Are you holding onto regret that's no longer serving you?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Decision quality over time

<SectionBanner why="The real measure isn't any single decision; it's whether you're getting better at deciding. These questions track the trend line." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Improvement is invisible without a baseline. Asking whether you're getting better forces you to compare your decisions across time instead of judging each in isolation."
  howOften="Quarterly, comparing recent calls to older ones"
  when="During a longer review of your judgment over the year"
  record="Note where your decision-making has clearly evolved">
  Are you getting better at making decisions? How has your decision-making evolved?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="You're better at some decisions than others. Knowing your strong and weak categories tells you where to lean on yourself and where to seek help."
  howOften="Quarterly, sorting your decisions by type"
  when="When deciding whether to handle a call alone or get support"
  record="Two lists: decision types you handle well, and ones you don't">
  What types of decisions are you good at? Which do you struggle with?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Reversals and course corrections

<SectionBanner why="Changing your mind is a skill, not a failure. These questions examine whether your reversals were wise and whether your timing on them was right." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A reversal is itself a decision worth judging. Reviewing the ones you've made teaches you when changing course is courage and when it's flinching."
  howOften="After you reverse a meaningful decision"
  when="When you've walked back a call and want to know if you were right to"
  record="Note the reversal and whether, in hindsight, it was the right move">
  What decisions have you reversed? Was reversing the right call?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The timing of a reversal matters as much as the reversal itself. Quitting too early wastes a good bet; quitting too late compounds a bad one."
  howOften="After a course correction, judging its timing"
  when="When reviewing whether you changed direction at the right moment"
  record="Write whether you reversed too early, too late, or on time">
  Did you reverse too early or too late?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="deep"
  why="The stay-or-pivot question is one of the hardest in any decision. Building a way to answer it keeps you from confusing perseverance with stubbornness."
  howOften="When you're caught between holding the line and changing tack"
  when="When a commitment is struggling and you can't tell if it needs more time or a new direction"
  record="Write the signals that would tell you to stay versus pivot">
  How do you know when to stay the course versus change direction?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Decisions avoided

<SectionBanner why="The decisions you keep dodging cost you quietly, every day they stay open. These questions drag the avoided ones into the light." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Avoided decisions don't disappear; they accrue interest. Naming what you're ducking, and why, is the first step to finally facing it."
  howOften="Weekly, scanning for the decision you keep stepping around"
  when="When something keeps showing up on your mind but never on your calendar"
  record="List the decisions you're avoiding and the honest reason for each">
  What decisions have you been avoiding? Why?
</Question>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Not deciding is a decision with its own price. Naming the cost of inaction often makes the choice feel less optional."
  howOften="Weekly, when an avoided decision lingers"
  when="When you're tempted to keep an option open just to avoid the discomfort of closing it"
  record="Write what indecision is costing you in time, energy, or options">
  What's the cost of not deciding?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Stuck decisions usually need one specific unlock. Asking what would help moves you from stewing to acting."
  howOften="When you're frozen on a particular decision"
  when="When you've named an avoided decision but still can't move on it"
  record="Write the one thing, information, conversation, or deadline, that would unstick you">
  What would help you move forward?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Involving others, in retrospect

<SectionBanner why="Who you brought in, and whether you actually listened, shapes outcomes as much as the call itself. These questions audit how you used the people around you." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="The right input at the right time changes a decision; the wrong input, or none, distorts it. Reviewing who you involved teaches you who to consult next time."
  howOften="After a decision where others' input mattered"
  when="When reflecting on a call that other people touched"
  record="Note who you consulted and whether you missed anyone you should have">
  Did you involve the right people? Should you have sought more input?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="medium"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Seeking advice and ignoring it is worse than not asking. Checking whether you actually listened reveals whether you wanted input or just validation."
  howOften="After a decision where you asked for advice"
  when="When you got counsel and want to know if you truly used it"
  record="Write what advice you received and whether you acted on it">
  Did you listen to the advice you were given?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Others' input can sharpen a decision or steer it off course. Tracing its effect on the outcome teaches you how much weight to give it next time."
  howOften="After a decision shaped by other people's views"
  when="When reviewing how external input changed where you landed"
  record="Note how each person's input moved the outcome, for better or worse">
  How did others' input affect the outcome?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
