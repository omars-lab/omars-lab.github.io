I used to think of "sales" as a department down the hall. It was something other people did so that I could stay in my editor and write code. Then I read Daniel Pink's *To Sell Is Human: The Surprising Truth About Moving Others*, and the wall I'd put between "building" and "selling" stopped making sense.

<!-- truncate -->

This isn't a summary of the book. It's the handful of ideas that stuck with me, in my own words, and how I've tried to use them. If they resonate, read Pink's book; the research and stories behind these points are his, and they're worth your time.

## The reframe: selling is moving people

Pink's central move is to redefine what selling even is. Underneath the cold-call caricature, "sales" is just the act of getting someone to part with something they value (time, attention, effort, money) in exchange for something they'll be better off having. By that definition, almost everything I do at work is selling. Convincing a team to adopt an approach, getting a reviewer to prioritize my PR, persuading a stakeholder that a refactor is worth the quarter: none of it exchanges money, but all of it is moving people.

Pink calls this **non-sales selling**, and his argument that we're all doing it now landed hard for me. Two of his reasons especially:

- **Small and flat organizations make everyone a seller.** In a big company, selling is a specialized function so that everyone else can specialize too. The smaller or flatter the org, the more those boundaries dissolve. Designers analyze, analysts design, and engineers end up influencing customers directly. I've watched this happen on my own teams: the further you get from a rigid org chart, the more "not my job" stops being a defense.
- **Information parity flipped the seller's role.** Pink borrows the economist's phrase *caveat venditor* (seller beware) to describe a world where the buyer can often know as much as the seller. When you can no longer win by hoarding information, your job changes from gatekeeper to guide. You stop being the person who has the facts and become the person who helps make sense of them.

The line that reframed my own work the most: the most effective way to sell a product is often to build something so genuinely useful, and to help people understand it so well, that they move themselves. "Products people buy" beat "products people are sold."

## The engineer's version: serve, don't pitch

The part of the book I keep coming back to as an engineer is the idea of putting builders in front of the people they build for. Pink describes "forward-deployed engineers": instead of routing every customer interaction through an account executive, you put real engineers in the field. They fix problems on the spot, and more importantly, they discover problems the customer didn't even know they had.

That matched something I'd felt but never named. The best "selling" I've done wasn't pitching at all. It was sitting with someone using the thing I built, watching where they got stuck, and fixing it. Interacting with people around their problems isn't selling in the brochure sense. But it sells. It's also just good engineering and good product work, which is why the distinction between the two has mostly collapsed for me.

## The three qualities I try to practice: attunement, buoyancy, clarity

Pink organizes the "how" of moving people into three qualities. I won't reproduce his framework in full, but here's what each one means to me in practice.

### Attunement: take the other person's perspective

Attunement is bringing yourself into harmony with the other person and the situation. The counterintuitive finding that stuck with me is that *power gets in the way* of this. The more authority you feel, the worse you get at seeing things from someone else's seat. So the move is to deliberately turn your power down: assume the smaller chair so the other person can take the bigger one, and lead with your ears instead of your mouth. As an engineer, my version of this is resisting the urge to explain my solution before I've actually understood their problem.

### Buoyancy: stay afloat in a sea of rejection

Buoyancy is how you handle the steady drip of "no." Three pieces of it changed my habits:

- **Ask, don't assert.** Before a hard conversation, interrogative self-talk ("Can I move these people?") beats declarative pep talk ("I'm going to crush this"), because a question makes you actually marshal your reasons.
- **Mind your ratio.** Positive emotion needs to outweigh negative by a healthy margin, but not infinitely; a little negativity keeps you honest and tells you when you're on the wrong path.
- **Explain setbacks as temporary, specific, and external.** How you narrate a rejection determines whether you persist. A bad outcome that's "this one thing, this once" is survivable in a way that "everything, always, because of me" is not.

### Clarity: help people find the right problem

Clarity is the ability to make sense of a murky situation and help others see what's worth doing. In a world where information is everywhere, the scarce skill isn't access, it's framing: surfacing the problem someone should be solving, not just answering the question they walked in with. That's curation, not gatekeeping, and it's most of what I think senior engineering and product judgment actually is.

## Why this stuck

What I took from *To Sell Is Human* wasn't a set of closing techniques. It was permission to drop a false distinction. I'm not a builder who occasionally has to suffer through selling. The building and the moving-people are the same job, done well. Pink's framing (attunement, buoyancy, clarity, and a posture of service over pitch) gave me a vocabulary for something the best engineers and PMs I know already do instinctively.

If any of this resonates, the book is worth reading in full. The ideas are Daniel Pink's; the stubborn wall I had to take down was mine.
