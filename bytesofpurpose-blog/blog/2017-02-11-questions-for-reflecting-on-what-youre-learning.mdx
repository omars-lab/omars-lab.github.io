It's easy to keep learning and never stop to ask whether you're learning the right things. Most of us absorb whatever lands in front of us instead of choosing what to study on purpose. These are the questions I return to when I want to step back, check that my learning is pointed at who I want to become, and notice the gaps I keep avoiding. Pick the ones that make you pause.

<!-- truncate -->

## Core learning questions

<SectionBanner why="It's easy to keep learning and never ask whether you're learning the right things. These questions point your studying at who you actually want to become." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Learning without reflection is just consumption. Pausing to name what you've taken in is what turns information into understanding."
  howOften="Weekly, while what you've learned is still fresh"
  when="At the end of a week of study, reading, or work"
  record="Write a few lines on what landed and what it changed in your thinking">
  What reflections have you had on the things you are learning?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Your past learning goals trace the shape of who you were becoming. Revisiting them shows how your interests have drifted and what you've left unfinished."
  howOften="Quarterly, looking back across your learning history"
  when="When reflecting on how your curiosity has shifted over time"
  record="Note what you wanted to learn at each stage and whether you did">
  What did you want to learn at different points in time?
</Question>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="You can spend years learning things that don't move you toward who you want to be. This is the question that catches misdirected effort before it costs you more time."
  howOften="Quarterly, as a directional audit of your learning"
  when="When you suspect your studying has drifted from what matters"
  record="Write an honest yes or no, with the evidence behind it">
  Are you learning the right things?
</Question>

<Question
  power="anvil"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Learning that aligns with your purpose compounds; learning that doesn't just fills time. Checking the alignment keeps your effort pointed at something larger."
  howOften="Quarterly, mapping your learning against your purpose"
  when="When deciding what to study next or whether to continue something"
  record="Note which of your current learning threads actually serve your purpose">
  Do the things you are learning align to your purpose?
</Question>

<Question
  power="chisel"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Becoming who you want to be requires learning chosen on purpose, not by accident. This question asks you to build the filter that keeps your learning aimed."
  howOften="Quarterly, or when designing your learning plan"
  when="When you're deciding how to direct your study rather than letting it drift"
  record="Write the test you'll use to choose what's worth learning">
  How do you ensure you are learning things that fulfill what you want to become?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Education focus

<SectionBanner why="Attention is finite, so what you choose to study is itself a decision worth making well. These questions help you aim your education instead of scattering it." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="A scattered education produces shallow range. Naming a focus is what lets your learning go deep enough to matter."
  howOften="Quarterly, when setting your learning direction"
  when="When your studying feels spread too thin to stick"
  record="Write the one or two areas worth concentrating your education on">
  What should you focus your education on?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Your curiosity is a signal worth capturing before it fades. Keeping a list of topics you want to explore turns passing interest into a plan."
  howOften="Monthly, as new curiosities surface"
  when="When a topic keeps catching your attention"
  record="Keep a running list of topics you want to learn more about">
  What topics do you want to learn more about? What do you want to learn about?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="quick"
  why="What you enjoy learning is often where you'll go furthest. Noticing it helps you lean into your natural energy instead of grinding against it."
  howOften="Quarterly, noticing where your learning feels effortless"
  when="When choosing between subjects to invest your time in"
  record="Note the subjects that energize you rather than drain you">
  What subjects do you enjoy learning about?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Growth areas and shortcomings

<SectionBanner why="The gaps you avoid looking at are the ones that hold you back the most. These questions name what you'd rather not, so you can actually work on it." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="fire"
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="You can't improve what you won't name. Asking where you can grow, plainly, is what turns vague dissatisfaction into a workable list."
  howOften="Quarterly, as an honest growth audit"
  when="During a review of where you want to be stronger"
  record="List the areas of your life and yourself most worth improving">
  What areas in your life can you improve? What areas of yourself should you work on?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="monthly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Naming what you're actively working on keeps your growth deliberate instead of drifting. It's the difference between improving and just hoping to."
  howOften="Monthly, tracking your current growth efforts"
  when="During a monthly check on where you're putting growth energy"
  record="Note the specific aspects you're currently trying to improve">
  What aspects of your life are you currently trying to improve?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Knowing you want to grow is different from knowing how. This question pushes from intention into an actual direction for change."
  howOften="Quarterly, after naming where you want to grow"
  when="When you've identified a growth area but not the path through it"
  record="Write the concrete way you intend to grow in each area">
  How should you grow?
</Question>

<Question
  power={['fire', 'chisel']}
  priority="high"
  cron="quarterly"
  depth="deep"
  why="Your gaps and shortcomings are uncomfortable to face but cheap to ignore at your own expense. Naming them honestly is the hardest and most useful move here."
  howOften="Quarterly, with deliberate honesty"
  when="When you're ready to look squarely at what you're missing"
  record="List your gaps and shortcomings plainly, without softening them">
  What are your gaps? What are your shortcomings?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Learning style

<SectionBanner why="How you learn best shapes how fast and how deeply you learn at all. Knowing your style lets you stop fighting your own wiring." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  priority="low"
  cron="adhoc"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Forcing yourself to learn against your grain wastes effort. Knowing your style lets you choose methods that actually stick for you."
  howOften="When picking how to approach a new subject"
  when="When deciding between reading, doing, watching, or teaching to learn something"
  record="Note which learning methods consistently work best for you">
  What is your learning style?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>

## Capturing realizations

<SectionBanner why="Insights are easy to have and easy to lose. These questions are about catching your realizations before they slip away unrecorded." />

<QuestionSection>

<Question
  power="spark"
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="A realization you don't write down rarely survives the week. Capturing them is what compounds insight over time instead of relearning it."
  howOften="Weekly, sweeping up what occurred to you"
  when="At the end of a week, or right after something clicks"
  record="Keep a running note of realizations as they happen">
  What realizations have you had?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="quick"
  why="Some learnings are too valuable to leave in your head. Asking what needs capturing forces you to act before the insight fades."
  howOften="Weekly, scanning for what's worth preserving"
  when="When you've learned something you don't want to lose"
  record="Write down the learnings worth keeping in a durable place">
  What learnings need to be captured?
</Question>

<Question
  priority="medium"
  cron="weekly"
  depth="moderate"
  why="Experience is the richest teacher, but only if you mine it. Asking what insights have emerged turns lived events into lasting lessons."
  howOften="Weekly, reflecting on what your experiences taught you"
  when="After a meaningful experience, while it's still vivid"
  record="Write the insight each experience produced, not just what happened">
  What insights have emerged from your experiences?
</Question>

</QuestionSection>
