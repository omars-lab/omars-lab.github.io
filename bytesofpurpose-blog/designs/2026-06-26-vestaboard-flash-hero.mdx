The homepage hero asks one question: **which door into my world do you want?** The answer became a
**departures board**. An arched window (a portal you travel through) shows a scene; a flash of light
fills the arch; the scene changes; and a split-flap **Vestaboard** beneath it rolls through the
alphabet to name where this gate goes. It is the A/B "test" arm of the hero experiment (the control
is a calmer scrolling card strip).

<!-- truncate -->

## 1. The concept

The pairing carries the whole idea: an **arched portrait window** says "a curated destination," and a
mechanical **split-flap board** says "departures, now boarding." Together they read as *a place you
travel to*, not a list of links. The hero is ONE click target (the whole gate is a `<Link>`); the
flash + board just animate which destination it points at.

## 2. The split-flap board (the SplitFlap component)

A real Vestaboard is a grid of character flaps. Each flap is on a drum and **rolls THROUGH the
alphabet** to reach its letter, so changing a word looks like a cascade of spinning flaps settling
into place. The component reproduces that:

- **A character deck.** `DECK = ' A..Z0..9 punctuation'`. To go from `D` to `G`, the cell flips
  `D → E → F → G`, one flap per step, not a single direct swap. That intermediate roll is the
  signature of a departure board.
- **Synchronized arrival.** A cell with more steps to travel flips FASTER per step, so every cell in
  the message lands on its target at the SAME moment. Per-step duration is `settleMs / stepCount`,
  clamped, so the board settles cleanly instead of finishing raggedly.
- **A fixed grid with filler.** The title word-wraps into a fixed `columns × rows` grid, centered,
  with the empty cells rendered as **blank dark flap tiles** (a real board is always full). The grid
  never changes size between messages, so the board's footprint is constant; only the flaps move.
- **The housing.** The tiles sit in a dark physical **bezel** with an inner recess + drop shadow, so
  it reads as one real unit rather than loose letters. Widening the board adds more filler COLUMNS,
  never stretches the bezel (the border stays the same thickness).
- **The flap geometry.** Each tile splits at the middle; a folding-down top leaf (old glyph) and a
  folding-up bottom leaf (new glyph) animate the turn in 3D (`perspective` on the cell, `rotateX` on
  the leaves). The leaves are **solid opaque** dark faces, which matters (see the bugs below).

## 3. The camera flash, bound to the arch

When the scene changes, a white light **blooms inside the arch and fills it**, masking the swap, like
a photo being taken and the subject changing. Two details made it work:

- **Bound to the EXACT arch.** The light overlay is masked with `arch-inner.png` (the precise arch
  silhouette, interior filled black, used as a luminance `mask-image`). So the light is clipped to the
  real arched window, edge-exact, on every card, including the base lip. A `border-radius`
  approximation never lined up with the drawn frame; the mask does.
- **Light OVER the scene, not a white-out.** The bloom is a **semi-transparent** white radial (peak
  alpha well below 1.0, layered stops). Even at the brightest moment you still see the figure, the
  frame, the depth. The first attempts went fully opaque white and the scene vanished into a flat
  void; pulling the peak alpha down fixed it.

The flip starts a beat before the flash, and the flash peak straddles the swap, so the brightest
instant hides the mechanical change. `←`/`→` arrow keys (a global listener) hop to the previous/next
destination, each playing the full flash + roll.

## 4. The bugs worth remembering

Two subtle ones, both about **GPU compositing layers**:

- **The white split-flap hairlines.** Mid-flip, faint white lines appeared between the flap halves.
  Cause: the leaves had transparent gaps, so the bright tile behind showed through the hinge. Fix:
  make every leaf a **solid opaque** dark face. No gap, no bleed.
- **The "line that comes and goes."** A faint vertical line appeared in the hero, to the LEFT of the
  portal, ONLY on a hi-DPI (retina) screen, and ONLY while the flash animated. No DOM element had a
  border there. Cause: the flash overlay's `mix-blend-mode` / `filter` / `transform` animation forces
  it onto its own GPU compositing layer, whose fractional-pixel edge paints a seam that can render
  OUTSIDE the element, and the hero's `overflow: hidden` turned that into a hard line. Fix: contain
  the animation with `contain: paint` on the portal box (and drop the unnecessary blend/filter), so
  the layer can't seam into the hero. The tell: it vanished at `devicePixelRatio = 1`.

## 5. How it's wired (A/B/C)

The hero animation is a PostHog feature-flag experiment, `homepage-hero-anim`: `control` = the
scrolling strip, `test` = this departure-board hero, `variant_c` = a train-station re-staging
(section 6). All three arms show the SAME destinations and copy; only the presentation differs, so a
difference in chooser-card clicks is attributable to the animation. A `hero card clicked` event (with
the variant + destination) is the conversion; exposure is the flag read. The design + hypothesis live
in the experiment post `/initiatives/homepage-hero-anim`.

## 6. Variant C: the same board, hung in a train station

The third arm keeps the portal + Vestaboard but changes the staging: instead of a flash, it asks
*what if you were standing in a station, watching a train pull through to the next stop?* Two moves
carry it, both CSS-only and reusing the same scene art:

- **The board HANGS.** The departure board is suspended from a station bracket: a brushed-metal mount
  bar with two cables dropping to the board, a grounding drop shadow, and a very subtle idle SWAY
  (a `transform-origin` at the mount + a low-amplitude `ease-in-out alternate` keyframe). The sway is
  about 0.6 degrees, enough to read as "hanging" without being a distraction.
- **The scene SLIDES like a train.** The active scene slides off to the left as the next slides in
  from the right (or the reverse, depending on direction), so for a moment both scenes are in the
  window together, like one train leaving as another arrives. A horizontal motion **blur**, synced to
  the slide, peaks at the midpoint and returns to zero at both ends; there is no flash, the slide
  carries the whole transition.

The retina compositing-seam lesson from section 4 applied directly here: the slide's `filter:
blur()` is exactly the kind of GPU-layer effect that seams on hi-DPI. So the sliding scenes live in
an `isolation: isolate`, `overflow: hidden` portal box (its own stacking context, clipped), and the
blur returns to zero at rest so no blurred layer lingers between slides. Verified clean at
`devicePixelRatio = 2` mid-slide.

This first pass is deliberately minimal: the slide and the hang. A backlog of station DRESSING (an
iron train-shed window frame, a warm platform-amber glow, a station clock) is kept as a swappable
layer, since the slide + hang mechanics are theme-agnostic and could go either Victorian or modern.

## 7. What I'd reuse

The `SplitFlap` component (`src/components/SplitFlap`) is the reusable piece: a Vestaboard display
that takes text, word-wraps into a fixed grid, and rolls through a character deck with synchronized
arrival. The arch-mask flash and the gate composition are homepage-specific, but the "bind a light to
an exact shape with a luminance PNG mask" trick generalizes to any shaped reveal.
