I had a column of zeros and a question. The [Gold Ionic binary-pyramid logo](/designs/design-binary-pyramid-logo) draws each **0** as a little gold ring stacked up the shaft of a column. What if I took one of those columns of 0s and bent it into a circle, so the chain of zeros became a **rosette**? And what if, instead of hand-drawing it, I built it the way the rest of this project builds Islamic geometry: by writing a recipe in a tiny drawing language and letting a CLI render it, then handing the result to a second CLI to check?

## The constraint that shaped everything: no letters

The first decision made itself. I wanted the zeros to be real geometry, not a typed character "0" in some font. That is also the only thing the tools can do: **bikar**, the renderer, has no text primitive at all. There is no way to stamp a glyph. A "0" has to be a *ring* drawn with a compass: a circle, or two circles, or an arc that closes on itself. So the rule "no letters in the SVG" is not a restriction I imposed on top of the tools. It is baked into them. Every zero you see below is a circle, and I checked the output to be sure: the final SVG contains zero `<text>` elements.

## The two tools

Two small command-line tools do all the work, and they pass the drawing between them:

- **bikar** takes a recipe written in a small drawing language (a DSL, a domain-specific language) and renders it to SVG. It knows about circles, divisions of a circle, arcs, polygons, and rotational repetition.
- **qiyas** (Arabic for "measurement") does the opposite. You hand it a drawing and it writes down what it sees: every shape, and the symmetry of the whole. It is the honesty check on whether the picture says what I meant it to say.

The plan: write a bikar recipe for a ring of zeros, render it, then ask qiyas whether it reads as an 8-fold figure.

## The recipe, and the iterations it took

The idea is one move: take eight points evenly spaced around a circle, and put a small ring (a "0") at each one. In bikar's language, "eight evenly spaced points" is `divide C0 into 8`, and the natural way to repeat a shape around a center is the `rotate` block. So my first recipe put a circle inside a `rotate 8` block and expected eight of them.

It rendered **two** circles, not eight.

That was the first lesson, and a real one. bikar's `rotate` replicates *drawn edges*: line segments, arcs, and filled faces. It does **not** replicate a bare construction *circle*. A circle in bikar is scaffolding, the thing you hang points and arcs on, and scaffolding does not get copied by a rotation. So my eight-fold block produced the original circle plus one rotated nothing.

My second and third attempts tried to fix that by drawing each zero as an **arc** instead, since arcs *do* get replicated. I tried a full-circle arc that started and ended at the same point. bikar drew nothing, because an arc from a point back to itself is ambiguous: there is no way to know which way around the circle it should sweep. I split it into two half-arcs, the way bikar's own rosette fixtures do. Still nothing visible, because a lone arc is not a closed face, and bikar only fills closed regions.

At that point I stopped fighting the `rotate` block and used the thing bikar was quietly telling me to use the whole time: a circle, on its own, already renders as a ring outline. A circle *is* a zero. So the recipe became eight named circles, one at each division point, plus a line that colors them gold:

```
blueprint rz
  circle C0 center(0,0) radius 100
  divide C0 into 8
  circle Z0 center(C0.cpt0) radius 40
  circle Z1 center(C0.cpt1) radius 40
  circle Z2 center(C0.cpt2) radius 40
  circle Z3 center(C0.cpt3) radius 40
  circle Z4 center(C0.cpt4) radius 40
  circle Z5 center(C0.cpt5) radius 40
  circle Z6 center(C0.cpt6) radius 40
  circle Z7 center(C0.cpt7) radius 40

pattern rz on rz
  circles color #E8C46A width 7
```

`C0` is the big ring the zeros sit on. `divide C0 into 8` gives me eight seats named `C0.cpt0` through `C0.cpt7`. Each `Z` is a zero centered on a seat. That is the whole recipe, and it renders the first time.

The only knob left was the radius of each zero, and it turned out to be the design decision. The honest log:

| Try | What I changed | What happened |
|-----|----------------|---------------|
| 1 | `circle` inside `rotate 8` | 2 circles, not 8 (`rotate` does not copy circles) |
| 2 | full-circle arc, rotated | nothing drawn (degenerate self-closing arc) |
| 3 | two half-arcs, rotated | nothing drawn (an open arc is not a fillable face) |
| 4 | 8 plain circles, radius 26 | works; zeros sit apart, beaded on the ring |
| 5 | 8 plain circles, radius 40 | works; zeros **overlap into an interlaced chain** |

Radius 26 reads as eight separate beads threaded on a necklace. Radius 40 lets the zeros overlap their neighbors, and the overlaps weave the eight rings into one continuous chain with a crisp 8-pointed star of empty space at the center. The second one is the rosette. Here are both, side by side:

## What qiyas saw

Then the honesty check. I handed the final SVG to qiyas and asked it to encode what it found. Its verdict, verbatim from the run: **9 shapes, dominant fold = 8, confidence 0.94.** It independently recovered the eight-fold rotational symmetry I had built, with no hint from me. The round trip closed: bikar drew an eight-fold thing, and qiyas, looking only at the picture, said "this is eight-fold."

Two things it got *interestingly* wrong are worth more than the thing it got right.

First, qiyas labeled all nine shapes as plain **circles**. It has a dedicated "rosette" detector, and it did **not** fire. That detector is trained to see a rosette as a ring of *petals*, polygon-ish or star-ish shapes, not a ring of circles. So qiyas sees "eight circles arranged with eight-fold symmetry" but does not promote the gestalt to "a rosette." The symmetry is recovered; the higher-order name is not. That gap is the kind of thing this whole project keeps running into: the machine can measure a property exactly and still not have a word for the shape.

Second, and my favorite, I asked qiyas to compare the image **to itself** and score the match. A perfect copy should score a clean 1.0. It scored the symmetry a perfect 1.00 but the overall match only 0.73, and it reported a **45-degree rotation** between the image and its own twin. Forty-five degrees is exactly one eighth of a turn. Because the figure is eight-fold symmetric, it looks identical to a copy of itself rotated by one seat, so the matcher happily "aligned" the rosette to a rotated version of itself and then found that several rings no longer lined up. The very symmetry that makes the logo pleasing makes it ambiguous to a machine that is trying to pin down its orientation. A perfectly symmetric thing has no single "correct" rotation, and the tool's confusion is just that fact showing up as a number.

## What the CLIs made easy, and what they made honest

The easy part was the design space. Once I had the four-line recipe, exploring it was changing one number and re-rendering. No vector editor, no nudging anchor points. The radius sweep that found the interlaced look took seconds, and every result is reproducible from text.

The honest part was everything bikar refused to do. It would not let me fake a zero with a glyph, because it has no glyphs. It would not let me rotate a circle into eight, because a circle is scaffolding. Each refusal pushed me toward the construction the geometry actually wanted: eight real circles on eight real division points of a ninth. And qiyas refused to flatter me. It confirmed the eight-fold symmetry, declined to call it a rosette, and surfaced the orientation ambiguity that perfect symmetry creates. None of that is failure. It is the tools telling me the truth about the thing I drew, which is the entire reason to draw it this way instead of by hand.

A column of zeros became a ring of zeros, and two small programs argued me into the right one.
