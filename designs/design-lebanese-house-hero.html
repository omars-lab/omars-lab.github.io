The House Hero: a Lebanese home you scroll through

June 28, 2026 · 8 min read
Omar Eid
Principal AI Architect & Entrepreneur

The Vestaboard hero is a component: an arched portal, a camera flash, and a split-flap board that names the destination. This post is about the house I built around it, a traditional Lebanese central-hall home whose carved central door is the portal, with the board hanging above it. It is also where the hero stopped being a timed animation and became a scroll-driven journey: you scroll, and the door transforms into each part of my world in turn.

1. Influence: the traditional Lebanese central-hall home​

The facade is influenced by traditional Lebanese architecture, specifically the central-hall (or "central-hall house") form that defines so many Mount Lebanon and Beirut homes: a roughly square, symmetrical body under a pitched triangular roof of terracotta tile, with a triple-arch opening at its heart (the classic triple-arcade window, a taller central arch flanked by two shorter ones). Wrought-iron railings run across the base of the arches; the openings are framed in pale stone against an ochre or warm wall.

I kept the silhouette honest to that vernacular and let the palette and detailing do the rest:

  • a teal square body under a terracotta triangular roof (the proportion is the reference's roof-to-body ratio: the roof's height is about half the body's),
  • the triple arch, a taller carved central door between two shorter zellij windows,
  • gold outlines tracing the square and the triangle (they share the eave line, so the roof's base and the body's top read as one continuous gold edge), with a single gold hip line running from the roof's apex down to a point a fifth of the way in from the bottom-right corner,
  • gold balcony railings in front of the windows only (a door has no railing),

so the hero reads as a specific PLACE drawn from a real architectural tradition, not a generic storefront. The earlier stagings (a train station, then a Moroccan creative studio) were steps on the way to this one; the central-hall home is the version that stuck because the triple arch already gives you the "central door you travel through, flanked by windows" composition for free.

2. The staging: board over a carved central door​

The house reuses the Vestaboard + camera flash wholesale; the staging is what is new:

  • The board HANGS over the door. The split-flap board is suspended above the central door on a mount bar with short chains, a grounding drop shadow, so it reads as a real hanging sign on the wall below the roof's cornice rather than a label pasted on.
  • The central door is the portal. The carved door occupies the tall central arch. It is the one opening that transforms: it shows the door itself (board says WELCOME) or a project SCENE (board says the destination). The two flanking windows are static zellij decoration, so the eye knows where to look.
  • The flash is bound to the door's arch. The same arch-inner.png luminance mask from the Vestaboard post clips the white bloom to the central arch exactly, so the camera flash fills the doorway and nothing else.

3. How it's wired (A/B/C/D)​

The presentation is a PostHog feature-flag experiment, homepage-hero-anim. All arms show the SAME destinations and copy, only the staging differs, so a difference in chooser-card clicks is attributable to the presentation:

  • control = scroll, the seamless scrolling film strip,
  • test = flash, the bare departure-board portal (the Vestaboard post's hero),
  • variant_c = studio, which became this Lebanese central-hall house,
  • variant_d = boutique, a lit storefront with three glowing arches.

A hero card clicked event (with the variant + destination) is the conversion; exposure is the flag read. The design and hypothesis live in the experiment post /initiatives/homepage-hero-anim.

4. The parallax pivot: scroll DECIDES the journey​

Once the house facade settled, the hero stopped cycling on a timer and became scroll-driven. As you scroll the page, the central door transforms into each scene in turn, the board reflips to that scene's title, and scrolling itself decides which scenes you traverse (scroll down to go deeper into my world, scroll up to come back). The white flash stays, but it is now triggered by scroll position crossing each scene's threshold, not by a clock. The feel shifts from "a sign that changes on its own" to "a journey you drive with the wheel."

Three decisions defined the pivot:

  • All three scroll models ship, as A/B/C experiment arms. Rather than guess which scroll feel lands best, the pivot builds three and lets a flag pick:
    • pin (scroll-jack): the hero sticks full-screen inside a tall spacer; each scroll step advances one scene; once all seven are traversed the hero releases and the rest of the page scrolls normally. The strongest "travel through a sequence" feel.
    • inplace: the hero stays in normal document flow; the door morphs based on how far the hero has scrolled through the viewport. No scroll hijack, gentler, but the effect only spans the hero's own height.
    • horizontal: vertical scroll maps to a horizontal pan across the seven scenes inside a pinned hero (a horizontal-scroll gallery); the door shows the current scene in the pan. These are a SEPARATE experiment from the animation A/B in section 3, registered under their own flag key so the scroll-model question can be answered independently of the flash-vs-scroll question.
  • Keep the white flash, scroll-triggered. The per-scene transition is still the camera flash bound to the door's arch, so the pivot reuses the existing door-to-scene cross-fade and masked bloom layers wholesale; only the TRIGGER moves from a timer to a scroll-progress threshold.
  • The matching top navbar item highlights. The seven scenes map one-to-one to existing top-navbar destinations (/craft, /journey, /thoughts, /mindset, /initiatives, /questions, /designs), so as the door shows a scene its navbar item lights up. The homepage route is /, so Docusaurus marks nothing active by default, which means the hero can own that highlight cleanly without fighting the router. It reverts when the hero leaves the viewport.

Guardrails the pivot must hold: it must never permanently trap the wheel (pin and horizontal RELEASE after the last scene), prefers-reduced-motion drops the flash and sets the scene instantly, and the flashing arch keeps its isolation: isolate so the hi-DPI compositing seam from the Vestaboard post cannot return.

One progress model, two drivers (scroll OR self-running)​

The question that shaped the architecture was: how do all these pieces, the flash bloom, the door to scene swap, the split-flap board, change on SCROLL or on a self-running ANIMATION, without special-casing each? The answer is one idea applied everywhere: every animatable piece is a pure function of a single normalized progress p in [0,1] across all the scenes (deriveSceneState). That progress is supplied by one of two interchangeable drivers, and no component knows which it has:

  • the scroll driver maps p from scroll position, so the whole thing is fully SCRUBBABLE: scroll forward and the flash blooms and the board flips; scroll back and they reverse; STOP scrolling and everything FREEZES at that exact frame (you can hang on the flash mid-bloom, the board halts mid-flip);
  • the time driver ramps the same p on a requestAnimationFrame clock, so the non-scroll arm self-runs (hold a scene, cross to the next, loop), reusing the identical visuals.

Concretely the split-flap board grew a driven mode: given a progress plus the text it is flipping FROM, each flap freezes at that progress instead of running its own timer, so scroll position turns the flaps and stopping stops them. The flash is always one continuous opacity variable. Because the model is one pure function, the same house animates on a wheel or on a clock with no forked code.

The two-drivers design also made the default trivial to choose, and to change. The homepage currently DEFAULTS to the time driver (the self-running animation house cycles on its own clock, in normal page flow), with the three scroll-driven models kept reachable for A/B testing via the override ?ab-homepage-hero-scroll=pin|inplace|horizontal. Because both drivers feed the identical model, flipping the default between "animation" and "scroll" is a one-line routing change, not a rewrite.

5. What carried over, what's house-specific​

The board, the flash, and the arch mask are the reusable spine, documented in the Vestaboard hero post. House-specific are the central-hall facade (the teal body, terracotta roof, gold outlines, zellij windows, hanging sign), the A/B/C/D staging, and the scroll-driven parallax. The split keeps each post about one thing: the component you could lift into another project, and the place I built with it.
