What I Ask Myself: To Discover What Interests Me

February 11, 2017 · 14 min read
Omar Eid
Principal AI Architect & Entrepreneur

Here's the trap with "what are you interested in?" you can't list your interests on command. You don't magically know them. The honest way in is to infer them from what you already do, read, build, and avoid. These are the questions I use to reverse-engineer my own curiosity: what draws me, what I'm done with, and what problems I keep circling back to.

Inferring interests from what you do​

I can't recite my interests on demand, but my behavior leaks them constantly. These questions read the evidence I'm already producing instead of waiting for a list to appear.

Topics and interests​

The obvious layer is still worth naming: what topics actually draw me, and which ones have quietly stopped. Tracking the change tells me where I'm heading.

Problems and questions​

My deepest interests tend to show up as problems I can't stop poking at. These questions look for the puzzles I'm drawn to solve, which is often the most durable signal.

Resources and tools​

What I want to read and which tools I reach for are quiet votes for my interests. These questions surface the resources my curiosity is already pointing at.

Projects and research​

Projects are where interests get tested against reality. These questions help me pick the ones that pull several threads together and actually move me toward my goals.

Identifying your passions​

Passions are interests that have hardened into something I'll return to without prompting. These questions help me tell a real passion apart from a passing enthusiasm.

Identifying your hatreds​

My aversions point at my interests from the other side. I hate wasting time on redundant work, which is probably exactly why I'm drawn to automation, so what I can't stand is worth reading just as carefully as what I love.

Activities and themes that recur​

When I look across what I keep doing, the themes repeat. I want to build, to identify and solve problems and puzzles. I'm passionate about composing meaning, which may be why I'm pulled toward both engineering and arts and crafts. I like designing things I think are valuable and seeing if they work. Part of my passion is pioneering, mixing and matching ideas, being creative. The recurring theme is structuring and organizing a thinking process so it can help others structure their own thoughts.

Problems worth dedicating yourself to​

Some problems are big enough to organize a life around. These questions stress-test a problem against whether it's truly mine and whether enough people share it to matter.

Narrowing down​

Eventually a wide field of interests has to collapse into a direction. These questions help me find the roles, works, and domains where my interests concentrate.
