What I Ask Myself: To Manage My Tasks

February 13, 2017 · 13 min read
Omar Eid
Principal AI Architect & Entrepreneur

Tasks are the atoms of my work: the individual things that make up my plans and initiatives. But how I capture, track, and structure them determines whether my system actually serves me or just collects clutter. The questions below are prompts to interrogate my own task process: how tasks get in, how they move, what they depend on, and what could be automated.

Capturing tasks​

A task system is only as good as what makes it in. If capture is hard or unclear, half my work never reaches the list and lives in my head instead.

Tracking tasks​

Capture gets tasks in; tracking is how I know where each one stands and what to touch next. Without it, the list rots into a pile I avoid.

Immediate tasks​

Underneath tracking sits the now: the small set I can act on this instant. These keep me anchored to the next concrete action instead of the whole pile.

Desire to do​

Not everything I want to do has earned a task yet. Surfacing those wants keeps the wishlist honest and shows me when a desire has quietly died.

Capturing triggers​

Some tasks shouldn't live on a fixed date but should fire when something happens in the world. These questions are about catching those event-driven needs.

Recurring tasks​

A surprising share of my work repeats. Naming the recurring tasks (and dreaming up ways to regenerate them) keeps me from re-entering the same things forever.

Predicting potential tasks​

Beyond what already recurs, there's work I can see coming. These questions explore predicting tasks before they're due so they appear at the right moment on their own.

Task dependencies​

Tasks rarely stand alone. Many can't start until something else finishes or some event occurs, and modeling that is what keeps me from working things in the wrong order.

Automating tasks​

The least valuable work is the bookkeeping of tasks themselves. These questions hunt for what a script could create, recreate, or remind me of without my touch.

Breaking down work​

Some work is bigger than a task and deserves to be a story with a reason behind it. These questions are about decomposing effort and knowing why it's worth doing.

Structuring your tasks​

Tasks nest: subtasks roll up into tasks, tasks into projects, projects into goals. Seeing that hierarchy is what keeps a flat list from hiding the bigger picture.

Note: if weight is at least 1, the task is repetitive: it should happen around once a week.

The structure of my tasks is as follows:

Goal A
     Project A for Goal A
          Research A for Project A
          Task A for Project A
              Subtask A for Task A
         Subtask B for Project A
     Task B for Goal A
     Research B for Goal A
    Subtask C for Goal A
